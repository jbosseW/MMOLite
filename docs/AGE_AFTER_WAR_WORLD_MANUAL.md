# THE AGE AFTER THE ATLAS
## A World Manual for Players and Game Masters

*This document covers the world of Fortuna as it exists in Year 500 Post-Atlas — the present day of MMOLite. The continent of Fortuna. The Helios Doctrine. The Rift that was placed as divine punishment. The man inside it who has been trying to find his god for five hundred years.*

---

# CHAPTER 1: THE WORLD

## 1.1 Introduction

Five hundred years ago, a weapon called Heaven's Atlas was carried into the desert oasis city of Calidar — the joint capital of the Elven and Dark Elven peoples on the continent of Fortuna — and activated by a Dominion warrior who believed with his whole heart that he was doing something righteous. The weapon glowed sickly green and burned to the touch and was carried in a great metal container. It contained one of Helios's own organs. When the warrior placed it at the city's heart and activated it, Calidar was consumed in a hot flash of white light and sickly green fire. The marble colonnades and obsidian towers became green glass in seconds. The entirety of both Elven sub-peoples within the city were gone before the smoke rose.

A Dark Elven god punished the warrior. Implanted a malicious artifact in his chest — a task: five divine souls within the spatial rift the Atlas had torn open, five souls to retrieve. The warrior entered the rift. He spent what felt like aeons on its infinite shifting floors. He retrieved all five souls. The god took them, took the artifact, told him this was his eternal torment, and moved the rift to the old human capital as punishment for all its arrogance. The warrior has been inside it ever since.

The empire that emerged from that event banned magic. Not specific spells, not specific schools — all of it. The Luminary Inquest was founded in Year 1 to enforce the ban, with execution authority and no appeal process. Seven other peoples survived the Atlas event in various states of damage and demoralization. The Dominion's Helios Doctrine — the theological and political claim that humanity's demi-god Helios was the chief deity of all peoples on Fortuna, and that humanity was therefore divinely mandated to govern them all — became unassailable state policy. The High Elves, who survived Calidar's destruction only because they were not within the city when it detonated, began processing the paperwork that made everyone else's life legible — and therefore controllable — to the empire. The Dark Elves, blamed for having solicited divine intervention against the Dominion, were subjected to systematic persecution that reduced their population to near-extinction. The orcs, whose Great Khan had died in Year -45 with his succession clause unfinished, found themselves leaderless and under sustained imperial suppression. The goblins, who had no territory to lose, found themselves classified as a pest control problem in official documentation.

For five centuries, this arrangement held. It did not hold well. Then the rift placed by the Dark Elven god at the heart of the old human capital began to grow wider, and to spread outward — secondary rifts appearing in outlying towns, rift energy bleeding into the overworld, creatures pouring through. The entity inside the primary Rift — the warrior, changed by five centuries of divine punishment and the remnants of five defeated gods stitched into his limbs — has been pushing slivers of himself outward. He sends necromancers to tear the fabric of the world wider wherever his influence can reach. He is trying to find Helios.

He will never find Helios. Helios — the demi-god at the center of Dominion theology, son of a divine lord and a mortal woman — is sealed below the Dominion's capital, barely alive, his divine essence siphoned continuously by the Cardinals to power imperial artifacts. He has not been conscious in centuries. He cannot respond to prayer. He does not know the soldier who served him is looking for him.

That is the world in Year 500. The Adventure Guild — founded fifty years ago as the only institution all eight races can participate in without submitting to the Helios Doctrine — maintains ten anchor towns across Fortuna and manages what containment it can of an expanding crisis. You find yourself in one of the smaller towns outside the capital where a new rift has just formed. The Guild wants it contained. What is inside it is connected to what has been inside the primary Rift for five hundred years. Understanding the connection is the beginning of understanding everything.

## 1.2 Geography

### The Main Continent

The continent of Fortuna is approximately 10,000 kilometers east to west and 12,500 kilometers north to south. No single political entity controls more than a quarter of its landmass. The Holy Dominion's imperial claims cover more territory on paper than any other power, but the empire's actual administrative reach ends well before its declared borders. Everything beyond the fortified road networks, the cathedral cities, and the documented farmland is, in practical terms, contested or uncontrolled. The Dominion calls Fortuna the Enlightened Continent. Other races use the name without the attached theology.

The game world uses a chunk-based coordinate system. Chunks are 512 pixels wide, with one chunk representing approximately 5 kilometers at ground scale. The reference coordinate origin is at the Holy Dominion starter town, positioned at ref coordinates (35, 42). All other locations are described relative to this origin. Coordinates increase east and south; they decrease west and north. A player traveling from the starter town to Mechspire on the eastern islands covers approximately 57 ref units east — roughly 285 kilometers as a straight-line distance, considerably more by any actual route.

### Named Regions

**The Holy Dominion.** The central plains of Fortuna. Temperate farmland, fortified roads, and cathedral cities arranged around the Dominion's administrative and theological infrastructure. The empire's largest population center — approximately 1.5 million humans — occupies this territory, with the imperial capital Solara at the political and religious center. The Luminary Inquest operates out of the Cathedral District. Below the Cathedral of the Chosen, in vaults that have not been inspected by any independent authority in five centuries, Helios himself is sealed — alive, barely conscious, his divine essence siphoned continuously to power imperial artifacts. The Rift at the capital sits at the heart of the city the Dark Elven god chose as the location of humanity's punishment. The empire's official position is that the Rift is a divine testing ground.

**The Elven South.** Dense forest southeast of the Dominion heartlands. The elven Administration, integrated into imperial bureaucratic structures since Year 1, runs its records offices and archival holdings from the canopy cities here. Towering trees older than the Last World War, ley-line intersections that the magic ban does not fully suppress, and sealed archives containing everything the elves documented about Calidar before Year 0. Sylvaris is the largest city. The archives below it contain the original copies of Seren Vaelas's classified Rift documentation from Year 250, which the Inquest believes it suppressed and which the elves filed correctly under seventeen cross-references.

**The Dwarven Mountains.** Alpine peaks running across the north-central continent. The Free Holds of Stone are entirely underground — what the surface shows is rock face, sealed cave entrances, and the specific trade contact points where dwarves emerge on their own schedule to conduct commerce. The empire has been attempting to establish a formal military presence at the mountain approach for twenty years. The dwarves have not responded. Ironhold is the largest hold with surface-adjacent infrastructure; the deeper holds have not had an outsider inside them in living human memory.

**The Orcish Steppes.** Rolling grassland covering most of the western continent. The orcish clan confederation moves seasonally, with the larger encampments forming and dissolving based on grazing cycles and clan assembly schedules. Kragmor is the largest semi-permanent settlement — what the empire's intelligence reports call a "compliant seasonal settlement" and what everyone who has visited calls a war camp. Thousands of orcs alive today personally served under the Great Khan. They are patient in the specific way that people are patient when they have decided they can wait.

**The Gnomish Isles.** Temperate islands far to the east, past the Silver Seas. The Gnomish Collective's industrial capital Mechspire is inland; Clockwork Harbor is the port city through which almost all mainland contact passes. Access requires permits that are almost always denied. The mainland liaison station at Windtrap processes permit applications and sends most of them back. The Collective has assessed the Holy Dominion as a terminal failure state since Year 120 and has been operating on that assessment, quietly, ever since.

**Shadowfen.** A dense swamp in the southwest, classified by the empire as "uninhabitable exclusion zone." This classification is the official position and the Dominion knows it is inaccurate. Thousands of people live in the Shadow Fen Commune — refugees, unsanctioned mages, documentation-less drifters, anyone who cannot exist under imperial paperwork. The commune is protected by the Veil, a field of concentrated refugee magic reinforced by infernal pacts. Imperial cartographers draw the swamp as impassable. The infernal pacts are real; the empire is aware of them; the empire prefers the commune to remain classified as swamp because acknowledging it requires acknowledging what the Veil's infernal sponsors imply. Murkmire is the commune's largest settlement, built on stilts above standing water.

**The Wastes of Calidar.** South of the Elven South, extending deep into the southern continent. This is what Heaven's Atlas left behind. The glass is green — the sickly signature hue of the Atlas's divine-organic energy, fifty miles of it, still fluctuating in places where the magma beneath has not fully settled. The rivers are dry channels of fused stone where the oasis water once ran and boiled instantly. Once this was the most beautiful place on Fortuna: marble colonnades entwined with obsidian towers, a desert oasis where Elves and Dark Elves alike built their joint capital over five thousand years. The interior is not merely inhospitable; it is the most comprehensively lethal terrain on the continent. No shelter exists, no water table is accessible, no life larger than certain extremophile fungi has been documented past the border zone. Every surviving elf carries Calidar in cultural memory. The Ancient Ones speak of it in the present tense. The surviving Dark Elves carry it more personally still — they were the ones whose sect sought divine aid and whose gods, in responding, sealed the soldier's fate and their own extinction. Return is forbidden by imperial decree. The border draws scavengers attracted to residual divine discharge, which is the only reason the edge of the Wastes sees any human traffic at all.

**The Great Endless Desert.** Central-south interior. Ancient sand, buried cities, and the surface evidence of the lizardfolk's five-thousand-year presence on this continent. The Hidden River Empire — the lizardfolk civilization that migrated from the Subterranean Seas to the desert surface in pre-history — maintains its primary underground networks here. The surface ruins that imperial cartographers record as pre-war astronomical instruments are correct: the Coldwatch observatory dates to before any other race arrived on this continent. The observatory below the ruins is still operational.

**The Scorched Sands.** Western volcanic desert, distinct from the Wastes of Calidar. This is naturally hostile terrain: obsidian flows, active lava tubes, mineral deposits that attract mining operations every few decades until the heat drives them out. Some orcish clans use the fringes for coming-of-age trials. The goblin tunnel network at Redcap Junction runs under the imperial checkpoint that has been standing directly above it for sixty years.

**The Frostbound Reach.** Far north tundra. Extreme cold, minimal infrastructure, very few settlements. The gnomish survey network extends here with a northern observation station that watches the continent from above the desert's northern edge. Primarily significant as the outer limit of mapped territory.

**The Great Western Isle.** A large island approximately 30,000 square kilometers in area, positioned far to the west. Largely unmapped in current Guild records. Historical records reference it in connection with pre-war trade routes but contain no reliable post-war documentation.

**The Ashen Archipelago.** A volcanic island chain formed, at least partially, by the catastrophic volcanic event of Year minus-600 that reshaped the western ocean region. Earliest goblin diaspora patterns trace back to displaced populations from this area. Current status: sparsely inhabited, volcanically active.

**The Silver Seas and the Shimmering Sea.** The eastern waters between the main continent and the Gnomish Isles. Navigation is complicated by unpredictable currents and the Collective's patrol vessels, which enforce the permit system at sea as rigorously as Clockwork Harbor does on land.

## 1.3 The Imperial Documentation System

Every person living within the Holy Dominion's administered territories carries papers. This is not a suggestion. A person without papers is, in legal terms, a person who does not exist — which in practice means they can be detained, held indefinitely, or removed without any process whatsoever, because no process exists for a non-person. The system has operated this way since Year 5, when the Dominion's administrative council concluded that the magic ban required an enforcement mechanism that could track lineage, identify concealed magic users, and flag cross-racial cooperation that might constitute conspiracy. The documentation system is that mechanism. It was never intended as a public safety measure and has never functioned as one.

Every person's papers specify: identity (name, race, physical description), legal status (citizen, subject, restricted, provisional), magical lineage (three generations back, annotated for any documented magical ability), travel history (city of origin, current registered residence, any travel permits issued and their status), economic activity (registered employer, trade guild membership, property holdings, outstanding debts to imperial entities), and religious standing (registered faith, Helios observance status, any documented attendance at unsanctioned religious gatherings). Papers must be presented at city gates, bridge crossings, and random inspections conducted by Luminary Inquest officers or local garrison commanders. Officers have the authority to confiscate papers pending review. A person whose papers are confiscated pending review is in legal limbo with no defined timeline and no appeals process.

The system is administered primarily by elves. This is the central irony of its operation: the race whose homeland was destroyed by the empire, who survive under the empire's administration, are the people who run the paperwork that controls everyone else. The elves who administer the documentation system know every loophole, every vulnerability, every category classification that can be applied in more than one way. Certain files disappear. Certain travel permits are processed under the wrong category, which technically means they are valid for destinations they were not officially intended to cover. Certain individuals are recorded as having "returned to origin" when what actually happened is they were given a forty-eight-hour window and a route marked on paper in a specific way that experienced readers understand. The elves call this neither resistance nor collaboration. They call it precision.

The renewal cycle varies by race, which is a direct statement of institutional trust. Humans renew papers every five years — a comfortable administrative rhythm that most humans never consciously notice. Elves renew every twenty years, a concession to their lifespan made in the Year 12 administrative reform. Orcs renew every three years, a direct product of Year 400 restrictions imposed after the orcish elder assembly was suppressed and four elders died in imperial custody. Goblins renew annually and are required to appear in person at a Luminary Inquest office, which is a meaningful burden given that goblins typically lack fixed residences and must travel to comply. Cat folk renew every six months. The six-month cycle was established in Year 312 as a temporary measure during a civil unrest period; the emergency has never been officially rescinded.

The Shadow Fen Commune's position is different from all others. Papers presented at the commune's border are burned. Not confiscated, not filed, not returned — burned. The commune's founding document, ratified in Year 47 by the Shadow Fen Council, includes a single line on the subject: "Documentation issued by an occupying authority defines persons by the occupier's interest, not by any truth. We will not administer this definition." The infernal pacts that power the Veil protect the commune from the military enforcement that would otherwise follow from this position. The empire classifies Shadowfen as uninhabitable and processes no papers for it, which is the empire's way of declining to acknowledge that anyone lives there.

|------|-------------------------------|

## 2.1 Human

*"We are not the strongest race or the longest-lived or the most technically gifted. We are the ones who were standing when the smoke cleared. The Doctrine says this is divine providence. I have spent thirty years in the Inquest reviewing the files. I know what it actually was."*
— Auditor Cassia Vorn, Luminary Inquest (reassigned to the northern outpost, Year 461, circumstances not published)

### Overview

Humans are the dominant political race on Fortuna by virtue of having deployed Heaven's Atlas in Year 0 and written the subsequent history. The Helios Doctrine, the theological framework of the Holy Dominion, holds that Helios — the sun demi-god at the center of human religion — designated humanity as the rightful administrators of all other peoples. The Doctrine was formalized in Year 3 by the First Conclave of the Risen Light. The timing is notable: three years after the Atlas event, once it became clear the empire would face no military challenge to its position. The Doctrine provided theological justification for what was already a political reality.

What the Doctrine does not acknowledge — and what the elven archivists know and have never published — is that Helios is a demi-god: his father was a divine lord who lay with a mortal woman, and thus Helios was born mortal enough to be sealed, drained, and used as a power source. The Prophet-Kings have been drawing on that sealed power for five centuries. The Cardinals who know have never told the faithful. The divine warmth that the Doctrine's practitioners feel in their services is not Helios. It is the memory of Helios reflected back through centuries of ritual practice. Helios himself has not been conscious in a very long time.

The Dominion's civilian population is largely unaware of the full mechanics of its own power. Humans cycle through institutional memory every two or three generations, which means the empire is perpetually administered by people who were not present for the decisions they are implementing. The Inquest officer conducting a documentation check at a city gate in Year 500 is enforcing a system established five centuries ago, updated in Year 400 in direct response to the orcish elder suppression he has never heard of, and staffed at ground level by elves who know more about its vulnerabilities than he does. This is not an exception to how the empire works. It is how the empire works.

Within the Dominion's own population, a significant moderate faction has existed since approximately Year 300 — citizens, administrators, and clergy who believe the empire's enforcement scope has expanded beyond anything its founding documents justified. This faction does not have a name and is not organized. It exists as a recurring pattern in administrative decisions, documented dissent in internal correspondence, and specific Inquest officers who have a history of paperwork that disappears. The Adventure Guild has a disproportionate number of human members from this background.

### Society and Government

The Holy Dominion is a theocratic empire administered by a hierarchy that runs from the High Conclave of the Risen Light at the top through regional governors, garrison commanders, and Luminary Inquest officers to the local clergy who function as the empire's ground-level administrative presence. The High Conclave controls theological interpretation, which controls legal interpretation, which controls every other function of government. There is no secular branch.

Electoral and representative structures exist at the municipal level — city councils, guild assemblies, merchant boards — but these bodies have no authority over Inquest operations, military deployment, or documentation policy. They manage local infrastructure and dispute resolution within parameters set by regional governors. The practical effect is that human citizens have genuine local political participation and no meaningful national political voice. Most humans have never noticed this gap because the empire functions well enough, for humans, that noticing the gap requires deliberate effort.

The Dominion's military has not fought an external war since Year 0. It has conducted domestic suppressions, border enforcement actions, and what the official record calls "pacification operations" against several racial groups. The distinction between military and police functions collapsed approximately two centuries into the post-war period and has never been reconstructed.

### Relations with Other Races

Humans within the Dominion generally experience other races through the documentation system — as subjects requiring processing, as service providers, or as the exotic encountered on the road. The empire's educational system provides a version of racial history that is selective enough to be functionally false; the average educated Dominion citizen knows the Orc Khanate was a military threat that the war resolved and does not know about the Year 400 elder suppression, the goblin resistance network, or the infernal pacts underpinning the Shadowfen Commune's existence.

Outside the Dominion — in Guild anchor towns, in Rift operations, in the territories where imperial authority is thin — humans encounter other races on more level ground. Guild membership equalizes institutional standing by charter. Human characters outside their home territory discover that the deference they received from the documentation system does not follow them, which is either a shock or an education depending on the individual.

### Roleplaying Notes

- Human characters from the Dominion interior carry assumptions about institutional authority that other races do not share. The moment a human player assumes their Dominion status means something in a goblin warren or a dwarven hold, they have found their first meaningful cultural conflict.
- The moderate internal faction is a legitimate backstory option: a human who left the Inquest, a clergy member who started reading the documents they were supposed to file, an administrator's child who asked the wrong question.
- Human lifespan is the shortest of the eight playable races except cat folk and goblin. In conversations with elves, orcs, or lizardfolk, this is not abstract. An ancient elf NPC is speaking to a creature whose entire civilization exists within a portion of her living memory.

## 2.2 Elf (High Elf)

*"You ask how I keep the records straight after five hundred years. The same way I kept them straight after two hundred. The same way I will keep them straight after eight hundred, if the Rift permits it. Precision is not a talent. It is what you do when you have enough time to notice every error you have ever made — and enough reason to make sure the record survives longer than the people who made them."*
— Archivist Thessiel of Sylvaris, responding to a new Guild inductee's question, Year 480

### Overview

The High Elves of the Age After the Atlas are defined by two facts that exist in permanent tension: they are the most administratively capable race on Fortuna, and the state they administer destroyed their homeland. The surviving High Elves — those who were not within Calidar when the Atlas detonated — were absorbed into the Holy Dominion as an administrative caste. Their bureaucratic skill and extraordinary lifespans made them invaluable. The elven integration is the empire's most successful administrative achievement and its most morally incoherent one.

High Elves and Dark Elves shared Calidar. They were not separate peoples occupying the same city for administrative convenience — they were a single civilization that had grown into two sub-peoples through five centuries of theological disagreement about the Helios Doctrine. The High Elves counseled patience. The Dark Elves decided patience had failed and sought divine assistance. The Atlas did not distinguish between them. Both sub-peoples within the city died in the same moment. The High Elves who survived are haunted by the choice they made to wait, because it meant they survived and their counterparts did not.

Playable elves live between 500 and 800 years. This lifespan is long enough to remember Calidar through personal testimony — an elf born in Year 1 has spent five hundred years in the Dominion's administrative apparatus, beginning with the resettlement, living through every suppression and adjustment and political crisis. The living memory problem runs in both directions: elves know more about the empire's actual history than the empire's human administrators do, because elves were present for more of it and keep better records. This is leverage of a specific kind. The sealed file the elven archivists maintain — documenting that Helios is a demi-god, sealed below the capital, his essence siphoned for centuries — is the most consequential unpublished document on Fortuna. The elves who maintain it are still deciding when the time is right to use it.

The Ancient Ones are a separate population. Ten-thousand-year elves who survived Calidar, survived the resettlement, and are still alive in Year 500 are not playable characters. They exist in the world as NPCs of particular weight: figures who speak about pre-war Calidar with the familiarity of yesterday, who remember the civilization that died in the First Confluence, who know things about the Rift's nature that predate every other race's surface presence. They do not hold imperial positions. The empire made one attempt to employ an Ancient One in Year 23, which the Ancient One declined in a single sentence, and the empire did not repeat the attempt.

### Society and Government

The elven Administration is not a government in the traditional sense — it is the bureaucratic caste that the post-Atlas empire created and which has become, over five hundred years, as close to a racial institution as surviving High Elves have. The Administration's formal structure mirrors the Dominion's: regional archivists, document review boards, and records offices that process imperial paperwork. Its informal structure is something else entirely.

Elvish social organization runs on information. Who knows what, who has access to which records, which cross-references survive in sealed archives that imperial administrators have never looked at closely enough — these are the currencies of internal elven politics. Senior archivists are powerful not because of their rank but because of what they have filed where. An elf who has spent four centuries in a records office knows where the irregularities are, knows which files have been altered, and knows which alterations were made by whom. The Administration does not have a unified political agenda. It has individuals with specific knowledge, acting on specific opportunities, with plausible deniability maintained through meticulous paperwork.

The cultural wound of Calidar runs beneath all of this. Elves do not speak about Calidar casually in human company. Among themselves, in their own language, in the archival subculture of the Administration, Calidar is present in everything: in the specific taxonomy of loss that Elvish has for things destroyed rather than merely gone, in the way elves who never saw it still treat return to it as a matter of when rather than if, in the sealed sections of the archive that contain full documentation of the homeland that the empire burned and then forbade.

### Relations with Other Races

The elves' relationship with other races is shaped almost entirely by their position as the empire's administrative instrument. Every orc who has had a travel permit delayed knows it was processed through an elven office. Every goblin who has had papers confiscated knows the file was created by elven hands. The fact that some of those elves are working against the system as systematically as they can within it does not change the first impression, which is the one most people carry.

Within the Adventure Guild, elves are valued for their archival skills and their lifespan — an elven Guild member can build institutional memory across centuries in a way no human member can. Guild relations between elves and other races tend to be functional and careful. The deepest conflicts are with goblins, who have the most historical reason for distrust, and who know, specifically, that the paperwork that classified their communities as criminal was processed through elven offices. The deepest trust is with lizardfolk, who share an interest in long-term documentation and who have, for five millennia, been keeping records of things no one else thought to write down.

### Roleplaying Notes

- Playing an elf means playing inside the empire's most uncomfortable irony. There is no clean position here — elves survived by making themselves useful to the empire that destroyed them, and many elves have spent centuries asking themselves what that means.
- The archive connection is a genuine in-game advantage. Elven characters can access cross-references, sealed records, and historical documentation that other races cannot reach through normal channels.
- The frailty penalties are real in combat. Elves built for magic-based playstyles will rarely notice; elves who try to specialize in melee will feel the stat gap continuously.

## 2.3 Orc

*"The Khan unified forty-seven clans in twelve years. The empire suppressed the elder assembly in Year 400 because six clans met in one place and they knew what that meant. Draw your own conclusions about who they are afraid of."*
— Clan Elder Borrath, speaking at the Year 480 Rift Compact assembly, attributed

### Overview

The orcs have not had a unified government since the Great Khan died in Year minus-45, and the empire has worked deliberately to ensure that remains true. The Khanate, at its height, was the most effective military organization in recorded history — forty-seven clans unified under a single legal code, operating with a command structure that the empire's military historians have studied in classified documents for five centuries. The Khan died during the war's southern campaign. The cause is disputed. The succession clause in his legal code was unfinished at the time of his death, which meant no new Khan could be legitimately named, which meant the Khanate fractured into grieving clan confederations within a decade.

The orcs' defining characteristic, from the outside, is patience. Thousands of orcs alive in Year 500 personally served under the Great Khan. They remember the Khanate's legal code well enough to debate its finer points from memory. The Great Khan died five and a half centuries ago, and for an orc with a 300-to-500-year lifespan, that is approximately two lifetimes — not the remote historical past that it is for a human. The orcish clans have not reunified because every attempt at reunion since Year 0 has been met with immediate imperial military response. The suppression of Year 400, when four elders died in imperial custody, is not a historical footnote in the steppes; it is something specific people's grandparents witnessed.

What the orcs are building, in the interim, is what the empire cannot see: a philosophical and legal tradition that treats the Khan's unfinished succession clause as a technical problem awaiting a technical solution. Several scholars within the clan confederation have been working on that problem for centuries, in private, without producing anything the empire has found. Whether this is patience or preparation depends on who you ask.

### Society and Government

The orcish confederation has no central government. The clans — forty-seven at the Khanate's height, thirty-nine currently recognized under confederation law — govern themselves internally under their own chieftain structures and interact with other clans through a system of assemblies and councils that the Khan's legal code established as the mechanism for collective decision-making. The assemblies require quorum rules that the empire's military response to the Year 400 gathering disrupted permanently; holding a legal assembly now requires a specific set of conditions that has been met once in five centuries.

The steppes society that emerged from this structure is primarily oral in culture — the Khan's legal code exists in written form but survives primarily as memorized recitation, which the empire has found frustratingly impossible to suppress. Tent cities form and dissolve on seasonal patterns that double as military logistics, a fact the empire's analysts noted in Year 612 and have been unable to change. Horse culture is central to orcish military capacity: the steppes cavalry tradition is unbroken from the Khanate period, and every generation of clan warriors is trained to the same standard that the Khan's generals codified. The empire has never found a satisfactory way to address this in open documentation, so it does not appear in open documentation.

### Relations with Other Races

The orcs' relationship with the Holy Dominion is the defining relationship of orcish political life. Everything else is secondary to the fact that the empire suppressed the confederation, killed elders in custody, and maintains ongoing surveillance and travel restrictions on orcish movement. The clan chieftains who engage with Guild operations do so because the Guild's charter gives them institutional standing the Dominion will not, not because they have resolved their grievances with the empire.

Relations with dwarves are historically respectful — both cultures value practical achievement over formal authority, and the dwarven position of never-conquered resonates with orcish political aspirations. Relations with goblins are complicated by the same history: the goblin resistance's tactics and the orcish confederation's patient legal strategy are not in conflict, but they are not in coordination either, and both cultures have enough internal disagreement about the right approach that alliance has never been formalized.

### Roleplaying Notes

- Playing an orc in imperial territory means playing under the specific weight of enhanced documentation requirements. Pre-authorization for inter-regional travel is not flavor text. It creates genuine play friction in zones where orcs are stopped more frequently and travel permits are processed slowly.
- The Khan's living memory is present in orcish NPC dialogue throughout the steppes. An orc player character choosing to engage with that history will find it more specific and more recent than any other race's historical connections.
- The combat bonuses are the strongest melee-focused package in the game. Orc characters who build into the Khanate Vitality tree will have significant advantages in physical combat and long-session resource harvesting.

## 2.4 Dwarf

*"The surface people keep asking how we govern ourselves without a king. We keep explaining that we do not need a king because we are not afraid of each other. They keep asking the question, which tells you something about them."*
— Hold Steward Brekka Ironseam, during a Guild negotiation, Year 480

### Overview

The dwarves of the Free Holds of Stone have the simplest relationship with imperial authority of any race in the confederation: they do not have one. The holds are underground, the empire has no presence inside them, and the dwarves' position — that documentation requirements apply to persons traveling in imperial territory, and holds are not imperial territory — has never been tested militarily because the empire's military planners have never completed a satisfactory assessment of what assaulting the mountain holds would cost. The assessment has been requested three times by different regional governors. The third request came back with a two-word summary that the archives classify as "not suitable for general distribution."

Dwarven society is organized around labor and skill, not bloodline or divine authority. The Free Holds of Stone is an anarcho-syndicalist federation — each hold is governed by an assembly of its workers, organized by trade, with decisions made by direct vote among the members of the relevant guild. There is no central authority beyond the inter-hold assembly, which convenes annually at a location that rotates between holds and which makes decisions by unanimous consensus. Unanimity is slow. The dwarves regard this as a feature. A decision that requires unanimous agreement from thirty-nine hold assemblies is a decision that will be implemented with thirty-nine holds behind it.

The Deep Schism of Year minus-2000 is the defining internal divide of dwarven history: some dwarves refused to surface, descended into the hollow earth, and sealed the passages behind them. Whether those dwarves still exist, and in what form, is a subject that above-ground dwarven culture does not discuss with non-dwarves and discusses with evident unease among themselves. Guild dungeon documentation has encountered passage features consistent with dwarven construction in Rift floors that should not contain them. No Guild query to Ironhold on this subject has received a response.

### Society and Government

Each hold is self-governing. The guild assembly format means every major decision in a hold is a collective one — the master smith has authority over smithing matters, the miners' assembly votes on extraction plans, the engineering guild approves construction. No individual holds authority across guild lines. The position of Hold Steward is administrative rather than executive: a Steward facilitates the assembly process, maintains records, and represents the hold in inter-hold communications. Stewards do not give orders.

The inter-hold assembly meets annually and operates by consensus. This means dwarven external policy changes slowly and with broad buy-in when it changes. The decision to allow Guild anchor town presence at Ironhold took eleven years of assembly deliberation. The decision to permit non-dwarven access to a single specific tunnel level for Guild research purposes took twenty-three years. The dwarves regard this pacing as reasonable. The Guild, which operates on continental emergency timescales, has learned to submit proposals to the assembly well in advance and to wait.

Economic life in the holds is collectively organized. Mining output belongs to the hold's commons; individual dwarves receive labor shares based on hours worked and skill level, calibrated by the relevant guild assembly. Property within holds is leasehold rather than freehold — individual dwarves hold use rights, not ownership. Surface trade is conducted at specific contact points on schedules the holds set unilaterally. The empire's attempts to establish price controls on dwarven exports have produced no result except that some holds stopped attending certain market days.

### Relations with Other Races

Dwarves hold no systematic negative attitudes toward other races. This is not political neutrality — the dwarves have specific views about imperial authority that they will express directly if asked, and they have expressed them directly in Guild assembly sessions with sufficient clarity that the Dominion's Guild observer has filed three formal protests in eight years. But dwarven culture does not organize around racial hierarchy, which means individual dwarves in the world tend to interact with other races on the basis of what those races actually do rather than what category they belong to.

The closest inter-racial relationship in dwarven history is with the gnomes, whose collectivist economic organization the dwarves consider roughly correct even if the gnomish implementation prioritizes innovation in a way that dwarven culture finds slightly chaotic. The most difficult relationship is with the Holy Dominion's administrative structure, which dwarves in surface contact posts encounter as an entity that cannot accept the simple statement that the holds are not imperial territory and therefore the documentation requirements do not apply.

### Roleplaying Notes

- Dwarven characters entering imperial territory for the first time encounter the documentation system from the position of minimal compliance: technically legal, minimally cooperative, impossible to intimidate. The documentation check scene plays differently with a dwarf than with almost any other race.
- The darkvision and tremor sense are practically significant in dungeon play. Dwarven characters have genuine perceptual advantages underground that translate to combat positioning and exploration.
- The Stone-Born Artisan feat creates one of the strongest crafting specializations in the game. A dwarf who invests in mining and crafting skill trees will produce better material outputs than any equivalent non-dwarf.

## 2.5 Gnome

*"The Phase One assessment was completed in Year 120. The Phase One conclusion was that the Dominion would reach critical failure conditions within five centuries, barring significant internal reform. We are now 380 years past Year 120. The assessment has not required revision. The timeline is ahead of projection."*
— Production Council Communique 4, released Year 500, translated from Gnomish

### Overview

The Gnomish Collective is the most deliberately isolationist political entity in the known world, and it has been since Year 120 when the Collective's production councils conducted their first formal assessment of the Holy Dominion and concluded it was a terminal failure state. The councils did not publicize this assessment. They updated their operational posture, began a controlled withdrawal from mainland contact, and have been acting on that withdrawal plan quietly ever since. What remains of the Collective's mainland presence — Windtrap Station, Clockwork Harbor's carefully managed trade function — is exactly as much contact as serves the Collective's intelligence requirements. Not more.

Gnomish technology operates at a level that makes the Dominion's infrastructure look pre-industrial. The Collective solved the labor problem several centuries ago through automaton construction, which freed gnomish workers from repetitive industrial tasks and created a feedback loop of engineering advancement. Mechspire runs on clockwork systems that gnomish engineers rebuild and upgrade on a continuous schedule; Clockwork Harbor's automated loading equipment processes more cargo per hour than three comparable human ports combined. The Collective does not share this technology, not because they are secretive about having it, but because the production councils assessed in Year 340 that sharing it would destabilize the continental economy in ways that would benefit the Dominion more than anyone else. They did not share it.

Survey Station 3, established in Year 480 at the northern edge of the Frostbound Reach, has one specific operational mission: detect Heaven's Atlas-class energetic signatures. The gnomes are watching for the weapon that destroyed Calidar, which they believe may exist somewhere in a state of hidden storage or concealment. They have not detected it. This has not reassured the councils. The instrument that cannot detect Heaven's Atlas is either accurate (the weapon's components were destroyed or degraded below threshold) or inadequate (the weapon is shielded). The gnomish assessments of what Heaven's Atlas actually was — what it contained, and what its detonation did to the entity who carried it — are documented in sealed Collective intelligence files that the survey network's raw data has been informing for twenty years. Phase Three of the contingency plan was updated at an internal council session within the last decade. Three council members who would have opposed its original provisions were reassigned before the vote. What triggered the update is not in any document that has left the Collective's secure archives.

### Society and Government

The Collective is governed by production councils organized by industry sector. The council structure covers every major production category: agriculture, manufacturing, engineering, survey and intelligence, external affairs, and the defense council whose deliberations are not shared with non-council members. Each sector council operates by majority vote. The inter-council body that makes decisions across sectors operates by supermajority. No single gnome has executive authority over any other gnome's work domain.

Individual gnomes within the Collective hold their tools, their intellectual work, and their personal property. Productive infrastructure — the factories, the automaton workshops, the harbor equipment, the survey network — belongs to the Collective commons. Income from collective enterprise is distributed through a labor credit system that assigns value to work time and skill level as assessed by the relevant sector council. This system has been operational since before Year 0 and has been adjusted eleven times; the most recent adjustment was in Year 480, in response to automaton labor displacing certain categories of gnomish work in ways the original system had not accounted for.

Gnomish external affairs are managed by the trade representative assigned to Clockwork Harbor, who holds the only official contact role with non-gnomish governments that the Collective recognizes. The current trade representative is Kelva, who has held the position for forty years and has access to more gnomish internal information than any non-gnome alive — which still does not include the contents of Phase Three.

### Relations with Other Races

The Collective's official relationship with all other races is managed through trade. The trade relationship is real and actively maintained: gnomish precision instruments, clockwork components, and manufactured goods reach the continent through Clockwork Harbor in exchange for raw materials the islands do not produce. The relationship is commercial, not political. The gnomes will not sign onto political arrangements that commit Collective resources to continental problems; the Second Compact required significant internal debate and passed by a narrow margin, with multiple provisions specifically limiting what gnomish participation obligates the Collective to provide.

Within Guild operations, gnomish members are consistently assigned to technical and documentation roles that their Tinker Savant capabilities make them naturally suited for. Their relationships with individual members of other races are functional and occasionally warm; the Collective's institutional isolation does not transfer to individual gnomish personality. The cultural difficulty is that gnomes from Mechspire arrive in the wider world with an accurate understanding of how much their government knows about continental affairs and how little of that knowledge they are allowed to share. This creates a specific dynamic: gnomish characters who know things they cannot say, about matters they genuinely care about, in conversations where the information would be directly useful.

### Roleplaying Notes

- Gnomish characters in mainland settings carry the Collective's intelligence advantage and the Collective's communication restrictions simultaneously. Playing a gnome is partly playing the tension between what your character knows and what they are institutionally permitted to share.
- The Tinker Savant feat creates the strongest crafting output for mechanical items, automated systems, and precision instruments. A gnome who invests in cogworking will outperform every other race in that specific tree.
- Gnomish players in the Rift have institutional reasons to document thoroughly — the Collective wants Rift data. This creates a natural alignment between the gnomish racial identity and the adventure documentation culture the Guild promotes.

## 2.6 Goblin

*"My grandmother taught me the names of the forty who died in Year 412. Her grandmother taught her the names of the sixty who died in Year 303. The names go back to Year 0. We have not had enough years to forget them. We will not forget them until we have had enough years to do something about them instead."*
— Krix, goblin oral tradition keeper, approximate age 45

### Overview

The goblin resistance has been an active guerrilla network for five hundred years. It was not always called the resistance — in the early post-Atlas decades it was called the community, because that is what it was, before the Documentation Act of Year 12 classified goblin settlements as illegal encampments and their occupants as persons subject to removal on administrative grounds. The network that emerged from the subsequent decades of eviction, detention, and documentation confiscation is genuinely decentralized: cells of five to twenty, no central command, no permanent leadership, no headquarters. The empire calls it pest control. The goblins call it the only structure their circumstances permit. The Ironmother — who redesigned the resistance into its current cell-based form approximately two centuries ago — has no portrait in any archive, because the only people who knew what she looked like are dead, and she arranged it that way deliberately.

Goblin lifespan is 30-to-60 years. This is the shortest lifespan of any of the eight races except humans, and it creates a specific survival problem: how does a resistance movement sustain itself across 500 years when its members live an average of 45 years? The answer is oral tradition maintained with the rigor of legal documentation. Goblin oral tradition keepers — individuals specifically trained in memorization and transmission — carry the names of the dead, the specifics of every significant operation, and the locations of active tunnel networks in human memory because writing is dangerous and the empire burns books. The tradition has not broken in 500 years. Every generation teaches the names of what was taken and everything attached to those names, including the names of who took it.

BoneTrap, the goblin anchor town, is a warren in the steppes borderlands that outsiders are usually robbed upon entering. This is not a security failure. The entrance protocol is a first-pass evaluation of whether visitors are worth speaking to further. Visitors who respond to the robbery by attempting to leave receive their possessions back and a polite suggestion that they try Kragmor. Visitors who respond by demonstrating they understand the situation are eventually introduced to someone with actual authority, which takes longer and involves more discomfort, which is the point.

### Society and Government

The resistance has no government. It has cells and a tradition. Each cell operates independently with its own leadership, its own operational priorities, and its own local knowledge. Cells share information through a courier network that uses landmarks, dead drops, and coded oral messages rather than written correspondence. The network is robust against infiltration because no cell member has access to more than their immediate cell's information and one contact outside it.

The oral tradition keepers are not a governing body but they are an authority: keepers from different cells know the same core tradition because they were all trained from the same source material, which means they can authenticate each other and coordinate on matters of historical record. When cells disagree about facts — which happened during the Year 620 negotiations over the First Compact — the keepers convene and the tradition resolves it. This is not a perfect system. It is a system designed to function when every other system is being actively destroyed.

Goblin cultural values center on specific practical skills: tunnel construction, trap placement, supply caching, information security, and the specific kind of patience that comes from knowing you are not going to win on your timeline but your children might win on theirs. The goblin aesthetic — dark, cramped, efficient, frequently disguised as something else — is functional rather than decorative. BoneTrap looks like a deteriorating ruin from the outside because that is what keeps imperial patrols from looking more closely.

### Relations with Other Races

The goblins have useful working relationships with the Shadow Fen Commune (shared refugee network, tunnel access under the swamp), with certain orcish clan factions (shared enemies, shared territory in the borderlands), and with elven administrative sympathizers who occasionally make files disappear. The relationship with the Holy Dominion is not hostile — hostility implies the possibility of resolution. It is something more settled than that.

Within Guild operations, goblins tend toward roles that use their Guerrilla Instinct skills: dungeon scouting, trap detection, information gathering, and the specific set of social skills that involves knowing what people are not saying. Guild membership gives goblins institutional standing that no other structure provides, which is one of the reasons goblin Guild enrollment is disproportionately high relative to their population. The Guild's founding compact was signed in a format specifically designed to accommodate goblin objections: the copy the goblins signed does not appear on the same document as the Dominion's copy, and both copies are equally binding.

### Roleplaying Notes

- A goblin player in imperial zones is playing under a specific mechanical pressure: documentation requirements that create genuine friction, elevated NPC suspicion, and the daily renewable tension of existing in a system that classifies your community as illegal by default.
- The Guerrilla Instinct feat is the strongest stealth-and-rogue package in the game. A goblin who invests in thievery, lockpicking, and archery will outperform other races in those specific trees substantially.
- The oral tradition is a role-playing resource. Goblin characters have access to historical knowledge that official records suppress — the names of specific operations, the locations of tunnels, the specific circumstances of deaths that were classified as natural causes. This knowledge has genuine in-game utility.

## 2.7 Lizard Folk

*"You are asking me how old the Rift is. I will answer a different question: what is the oldest thing you know that is not the Rift? Because everything you just named, we were here before."*
— Sect Elder Vashari, declining to specify which sect, during the Year 480 Rift Compact session

### Overview

The lizardfolk are the longest-lived surface race on the continent, the oldest political presence on the continent, and the group with the most complete information about the Rift's actual nature. They say very little, and they are always specific when they say it. These two facts are related.

The lizardfolk's surface ancestors migrated from the Subterranean Seas approximately five thousand years ago. The Hidden River Empire — the lizardfolk civilization that exists mostly underground, organized along the Great Endless Desert's subsurface river networks — is older than every other political entity on the continent. The lizardfolk who are visible on the surface — the fifteen thousand or so who interact with other races, maintain Guild presence, and manage inter-racial commerce — are a small fraction of the total population, which is not known to anyone outside the lizardfolk sects. The empire's census lists 15,000. The lizardfolk have not corrected this figure.

Surface-visible lizardfolk are organized into knowledge sects: the Trade Routes Sect, the Astronomy Sect, the Burial Rites Sect, and several others that maintain less public profiles. Sects share information through private networks organized around the specific knowledge domain each sect manages. The Astronomy Sect founded the Veiled Hand in Year 1. The Trade Routes Sect manages the caravan network that maintains goblin and cat folk supply lines. The Burial Rites Sect documents the dead in a record that includes every significant death since the sect began keeping records, which they date to Year minus-4200. The Inquest has never accessed this archive. The archive knows about the Inquest.

### Society and Government

The lizardfolk sects are not a government. There is no lizardfolk state, no elected body, no administrative structure analogous to any other racial organization. The sects are knowledge-preservation institutions, each responsible for maintaining and transmitting the accumulated understanding in their domain. The Trade Routes Sect knows every caravan path across the desert, every water cache location, every safe shelter between surface points. The Astronomy Sect has celestial records going back four thousand years. The Burial Rites Sect has the dead. These are not metaphors. These are archives.

Sect membership is by invitation only and permanent. Lizardfolk are identified by the sect that trained them. There is no cross-sect authority; the sects cooperate when their interests align and operate independently when they do not. The Hidden River Empire that exists beneath the desert surface has its own governance structure that surface-visible lizardfolk reference obliquely and describe in detail to no one.

The Veiled Hand — the anti-escalation assassination network founded in Year 1 — was created by the Astronomy Sect after the sect's celestial record analysis at Year 0 produced the conclusion that the empire would repeat the Heaven's Atlas deployment if not specifically deterred. The Veiled Hand's founding mandate is narrow: remove individuals whose plans, if enacted, would cause mass atrocity. It does not have a broader political program. It does not have ranks or central command. It has the Astronomy Sect's assessment capacity and the patience of a culture that measures time in millennia.

### Relations with Other Races

The lizardfolk's relationships with other races are functional and deliberately maintained at arm's length. The Trade Routes Sect's caravan network serves goblin resistance supply lines, cat folk diaspora commerce, and Guild logistics across the desert without explicit political alliance. The Astronomy Sect's information network reaches every part of the continent without the sect ever claiming credit for what it knows. The practical effect is that lizardfolk characters tend to know things, and the challenge of playing one is deciding what to share.

The deepest inter-racial relationship is with elves, specifically with elven archivists who have been keeping records in their own tradition for five centuries. Both groups are in the business of preserving information that powerful entities would prefer destroyed. They have exchanged materials through Guild channels since the Guild's founding. They do not advertise this relationship. The most fraught relationship is with the Holy Dominion, specifically with the Luminary Inquest, whose history the Burial Rites Sect documents in detail and whose continued existence the Astronomy Sect considers the single most significant ongoing risk factor for a Heaven's Atlas recurrence.

### Roleplaying Notes

- Playing a lizardfolk character means playing someone who knows more about the world's deep history than any other playable race. The challenge is that this knowledge is sect-specific, not universal — a Trade Routes lizardfolk knows the continent's commercial networks in extraordinary depth, but may not know what the Astronomy Sect knows about the Rift.
- Thermal vision is a significant practical advantage in dungeon play. Seeing through walls and detecting heat signatures changes the information available to a lizardfolk character in every Rift floor.
- The ritual magic skill trees are powerful and race-locked. Lizardfolk players who invest in Ritual Magic, Water Rituals, and Blood Rituals access abilities that no other race can replicate and cannot trade away.

## 2.8 Cat Folk (Beast Folk)

*"The Salt Court has been meeting annually for at least two hundred years. The empire does not know it exists. I mention this because when people ask how the diaspora survives without a territory or a government, I want them to understand: we do not lack structure. We lack the kind of structure that needs to own land to function."*
— Trade Arbiter Mira of the Salt Court, Year 498

### Overview

The cat folk are the diaspora race. No territory, no government, no recognized legal standing in imperial courts. The documentation system marks them and goblins as the highest-risk categories for what the empire's internal reports call "processing irregularities." They carry multiple copies of their papers because confiscation is routine. They renew every six months because the empire has never officially ended the Year 312 emergency declaration that established that cycle.

What the cat folk have instead of territory is mobility and pattern recognition. The diaspora moves through desert trade routes, gambling towns, and commercial hubs across the continent, conducting commerce and maintaining their own legal traditions through the Salt Court — an annual gathering that has met for at least two centuries without imperial knowledge and has resolved every significant inter-community dispute in that time with a binding arbitration record. The Salt Court meets at Fortune's Rest, which the empire's maps describe as a desert gambling town. The maps are not wrong. They are incomplete.

Fortune's Rest is the closest thing the diaspora has to a fixed home, and it is fixed only in the sense that it is the place that various cat folk return to. The town is built on a desert oasis and runs on gambling, trade caravan commerce, and information brokerage. The information that passes through Fortune's Rest is valuable enough that the Veiled Hand has maintained a contact in the town since Year 340. Guild intelligence considers Fortune's Rest one of the best early-warning locations on the continent for political developments in the desert quadrant.

Cat folk lifespan is 60-80 years — longer than goblins but shorter than humans, which means the diaspora's cultural continuity works differently from the goblin oral tradition. Cat folk use written records, maintained carefully and carried in formats designed to survive confiscation. Every cat folk community of more than twenty keeps a community record. The community records use a writing system that Imperial linguists have not decoded, partly because the cipher is changed on a schedule the communities coordinate at the Salt Court.

### Society and Government

The cat folk diaspora governs itself through the Salt Court. The Court meets annually, draws attendance from every active diaspora community on the continent, and has authority over inter-community disputes, trade agreements, and the maintenance of the cipher used in community records. It is not a legislature and does not create new law; its function is arbitration and coordination. The arbitration decisions it produces are binding within diaspora communities through a social contract that has held for the duration of the Court's recorded existence, which the Court's own records date to Year 645.

Below the Salt Court, individual communities are governed by trade arbiters — experienced members chosen by community consensus for their knowledge of Salt Court precedent and their ability to mediate quickly. Trade arbiters are not permanent positions; they hold authority for disputes they are asked to resolve, not ongoing executive authority. A community of thirty might have a trade arbiter active for two weeks during a commercial dispute and then inactive for a year until the next one.

The commercial networks the diaspora maintains are the most extensive on the continent outside the gnomish logistics operation. Cat folk caravans cover the desert quadrant, the steppes borderlands, and the coastal trade routes with a consistency that has persisted despite documentation harassment, confiscation, and periodic route disruption. The fact that the diaspora has no territory to protect means that disrupting one route just moves the traffic somewhere else. This is understood by every experienced imperial administrator and regarded as the central intractable problem of cat folk policy, which is why the policy has not changed in five centuries.

### Relations with Other Races

The cat folk maintain working relationships with nearly every racial community on the continent, which is a function of commerce rather than politics. Trade arbiters from the Salt Court have worked with elven archivists on document safety protocols, with goblin resistance cells on supply caching along shared desert routes, and with lizardfolk Trade Routes Sect members on caravan coordination throughout the Great Endless Desert. These are commercial relationships, but they are sustained and trustworthy, which in the current political environment is a form of alliance.

The Dominion is the defining adversarial relationship, specifically through the documentation system's six-month renewal cycle and the elevated inspection rate. Cat folk players in imperial zones have more experience with documentation harassment than any non-goblin race. This has produced a very specific kind of political consciousness: not ideological, not abstractly radical, but deeply informed about how the system actually operates, where its pressure points are, and which specific administrators are worth knowing.

### Roleplaying Notes

- The Pattern Recognition feat is the strongest luck-modifying package in the game and creates significant gacha-adjacent gameplay effects: more card draws in the rare-and-above tier, more favorable random event outcomes, and enhanced card draw weights that players who invest in card collection will feel immediately.
- Cat folk characters in imperial zones play the documentation game more intensely than any other race except goblins. The six-month renewal cycle and elevated inspection rate are live gameplay mechanics, not background flavor.
- The darkvision and desert speed bonus make cat folk naturally effective in the Rift's deeper floors, which feature more darkness and arid themes at higher depth. Combined with the stealth bonus, a cat folk character who builds toward rogue playstyle has strong dungeon viability.

# CHAPTER 3: HISTORY

---

## 3.1 How to Read This Timeline

The history of this world is not a story of progress. It is a story of accumulation — of the consequences of specific decisions made by specific people piling up until the weight of them changes the shape of everything below. The Rift is not a mystery that appeared suddenly. It is the end state of a chain that starts before human civilization existed, runs through five thousand years of recorded history, and arrives at the present moment — Year 500 Post-Atlas — still moving. Players who understand that chain understand why nothing in the world is simple.

The timeline below is organized into eras. Entries are dated from Year 0, the year Heaven's Atlas destroyed Calidar and ended the Last World War. Years before that event are notated as Y-[number] (Y-500, Y-1000, and so on). Where exact years are disputed or reconstructed from indirect evidence, this is noted in the entry.

One important note on sources: the era before Year 0 is documented primarily from three places — the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect's celestial records, the elven archives of pre-war Calidar (fragmentary; most originals destroyed), and the Burial Rites Sect's death records, which establish dates for events by recording when the casualties from those events were registered. The Holy Dominion's official history begins at Year 1 and treats everything before that date as "the darkness before the Risen Light." Treat the Dominion's historical framing as propaganda unless corroborated by independent sources.

---

## 3.2 The Ancient World (Before Y-500)

### Y-5000 and Earlier: The Lizardfolk Surface Migration

The lizardfolk's ancestors migrated from the Subterranean Seas to the surface of the Great Endless Desert approximately five thousand years ago. The exact date is not agreed upon; the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect dates the migration to Y-5,200 based on celestial positioning records from that period, and the Burial Rites Sect's earliest surface death registrations date to approximately Y-4,900. The spread between those numbers likely reflects the gradual nature of the migration rather than a contradiction.

What the migration produced was the Hidden River Empire — a civilization organized along the subsurface river networks of the Great Endless Desert, with surface outposts at oasis points and underground city complexes accessible through cave entrances that are not marked on any imperial map. The Hidden River Empire is not a historical civilization. It is the current state of lizardfolk society, underground and continuing, with surface-visible lizardfolk representing a small fraction of its total population. The empire has been conducting operations beneath the desert since before any other race arrived on this continent.

The desert surface outpost settlements that eventually became the Coldwatch observatory complex date to approximately Y-4,800. The Coldwatch observatory is listed in imperial cartographic records as "pre-war astronomical ruins of unknown origin." The observatory below the ruins is still operational. These two facts are not in tension.

### Y-4000 to Y-3500: Elven Calidar at Its Height

The elven civilization of Calidar reached its peak between approximately Y-4,000 and Y-3,500. At its height, Calidar was a continent-spanning culture organized around its canopy cities, river archives, and the sustained magical scholarship that the elven lifespan makes possible in a way no other race's lifespan can support. The specific magical achievements of this period are documented in sealed sections of the Sylvaris archives that the Luminary Inquest believes it suppressed in Year 3 and that elven archivists have cross-filed under seventeen different reference systems since Year 5.

The continent-spanning nature of this culture is significant for what it explains about the First Confluence. Calidar's river archives included monitoring stations along the western coastal waterways. The records from those stations that survived Year 0 describe specific changes in the western ocean region beginning around Y-800 — changes in current, changes in thermal signature, changes in the behavior of wildlife near the coast. The Astronomy Sect has correlating celestial records. What neither record system describes is a cause, because the civilization that caused those changes was already gone.

### Y-3000: The Gnomish Collective Forms

The Gnomish Collective formed as a political entity on the eastern islands sometime around Y-3,000. The Collective's own founding records describe a process of collective economic organization that emerged organically from existing labor practices rather than a deliberate political act — there was no founding document, no revolution, no single moment when something changed. There was a gradual formalization of what the gnomish communities on the islands were already doing: treating productive infrastructure as commons, distributing labor income by hours worked and skill applied, making collective decisions by vote of the relevant workers.

The automaton technology that defines gnomish civilization was developed progressively over several centuries, not invented at a single moment. The earliest clockwork automatons that appear in Collective records date to approximately Y-2,800. By the time the Last World War began, the gnomish islands were running on automaton labor to a degree that had made manual industrial work largely optional. The production councils that govern the Collective today are direct institutional descendants of the councils that managed the transition from manual to automaton labor three thousand years ago.

The Collective has never claimed a mainland territory and has never wanted one. This is not passivity. It is a strategic position the production councils established early and have maintained consistently.

### Y-2000: The Dwarven Holds Deepen

Dwarven civilization on the northern mountains dates to approximately Y-2,000, though the dwarves themselves argue the date is later because earlier settlements do not meet the technical definition of a hold. The distinction matters to dwarven archivists and to no one else. What is agreed is that by Y-2,000 the holds were extensive underground city-complexes, the guild assembly system was operational, and the Free Holds of Stone existed as a functional political entity.

The Deep Schism of approximately Y-2,000 is the central unresolved fact of dwarven history. Some portion of the dwarven population — the records do not say how large a portion — refused to continue surfacing, descended into the hollow earth below the holds, and sealed the passages behind them. The above-ground dwarves have not publicly discussed what happened to those who descended. They do not discuss it with outsiders at all. Guild dungeon teams have encountered passage features in Rift floors consistent with dwarven construction, at depths that should not contain them. Ironhold has returned no queries on this subject. The queries are still filed. So are the descriptions of the passages.

### Y-1500: Orcish Consolidation Begins

The earliest precursors to the Khanate legal code date to approximately Y-1,500 — not a government yet, but a set of legal frameworks circulating among clan leadership that established a shared vocabulary for inter-clan dispute resolution, trade, and collective military action. The philosophical groundwork for what the Great Khan would eventually build was already present several centuries before the Khan was born. The Khan organized it and gave it force. He did not invent it from nothing.

The philosophical tradition underlying the Khanate code treats law as a technology for preventing conflict rather than a mechanism for enforcing authority. This is why the code's succession clause was treated as a technical problem by later scholars rather than a political crisis: in the Khanate framework, a succession clause is a specification document for a machine. If the specification is incomplete, the machine cannot run. The specification can be completed. The problem requires a solution, not a replacement of the system.

### Y-1000: Human Kingdoms Emerge

Human kingdoms began forming across the central plains around Y-1,000. The history of this period in imperial records is largely mythologized — the Dominion's official account describes a gradual spiritual awakening around Helios worship that unified the human peoples under divine guidance. The actual history, as preserved in elven archival records that cross-reference human population movements with corresponding documentation of conflicts, alliances, and displaced populations, is more straightforward: the central plains were contested territory, several kingdoms fought for control of the most productive agricultural land, and Helios worship consolidated from one of several competing sun cults into the dominant human religion because the most successful military coalition happened to practice it.

By Y-500, when the Last World War began, the central human kingdoms were not yet a unified empire. They became one during the war, which is the part of the founding story the Dominion's official history emphasizes. The mechanism by which unification was achieved during wartime is the part that receives less attention.

### Y-800 to Y-600: The First Confluence

The First Confluence is the name the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect uses for the collapse of an unknown civilization in the western ocean region between approximately Y-800 and Y-600. The word "Confluence" is the Sect's translation of a Draconic term that more precisely means something like "the moment when several things that were building separately arrive at the same point simultaneously."

What is known: between Y-800 and Y-600, something in the western ocean region ended. Multiple civilizations — no one has established how many — collapsed in a period short enough that the Astronomy Sect's records describe it as a single event rather than several unrelated ones. Calidar's river archive monitoring stations recorded the coastal changes described above. The goblin oral tradition has entries from this period that describe "the fire in the west that went into the ground" and "the days when the sea came up wrong." These oral records are imprecise but consistent across different cell traditions, which the tradition keepers regard as evidence of genuine historical transmission.

What the First Confluence produced: a wound in the boundary between the world's surface and what lies below it. This wound had no fixed location. It migrated. Over the fifteen hundred years between the First Confluence and Year 0, it appeared in different places at different times — always moving west to east, the Astronomy Sect's celestial correlations suggest, but never in the same place twice. The records are fragmentary enough that the Sect cannot definitively establish the migration pattern, only that multiple appearances at widely separated locations are consistent with a single migrating phenomenon rather than multiple separate phenomena.

The wound the Atlas fixed in Year 0 near the Cathedral District is the wound from the First Confluence. Heaven's Atlas did not create it. It pinned it.

## 3.3 The Last World War (Y-500 to Year 0)

### Y-500: The War Begins

The Last World War began as a series of escalating conflicts across the continent that crossed into continental-scale warfare when multiple factions began weaponizing magic at mass-casualty scales. The specific trigger for the initial escalation is disputed by every racial historical tradition except the lizardfolk, who describe it with the specific affect of parties who were not involved and are extremely tired of being asked to arbitrate between other races' accounts.

What is not disputed: by Y-500, every major racial power was committed to the conflict, magical reality was beginning to destabilize under the load of mass-deployment magic, and no faction had a clear path to victory through conventional means. The war had passed the point where winning was achievable through the mechanisms available at the war's beginning. The search for a decisive weapon was already underway in at least three separate research programs when the Great Khan unified the orcish clans.

### Y-200: The Great Khan Unifies the Clans

The Great Khan — whose personal name is known but not used in any formal orcish context; his title is sufficient — unified forty-seven orcish clans under a single legal code in approximately twelve years. This is the most significant organizational achievement in recorded military history. The mechanism was the legal code itself: rather than demanding submission, the Khan offered a framework that preserved clan governance internally while creating collective decision-making structures for matters that crossed clan lines. The code's genius was that it made unified action more efficient than fragmented action without requiring anyone to surrender internal authority. Forty-seven clan leaders signed it because their lawyers told them it was a good deal, and it was.

The Khanate's military capacity at its height was sufficient that every other faction in the war re-evaluated their strategy within two years of unification. The Holy Dominion's internal records from Y-185 describe the Khanate as "the single most significant military threat to the Dominion's continuation as a governing entity." This assessment appears three times in different forms in the same document, which historians take as evidence of how thoroughly the assessment alarmed the people who commissioned it.

The Great Khan died in Y-45, during the war's southern campaign. The cause of death is listed in contemporary records as wounds sustained in combat. The Veiled Hand's founding documents, written in Year 1 after the organization was formed, contain a single notation on the subject: "The timeline is consistent." They have never elaborated.

### Y-50: Heaven's Atlas

Heaven's Atlas was found or developed approximately fifty years before the war's end. The two possibilities — found versus developed — are not settled in any available record, and the distinction has significant implications. The lizardfolk Astronomy Sect's records from this period describe "unusual energetic signatures from the imperial capital inconsistent with known magical practice, consistent in type but not in scale with the structural wound phenomena observed in the western region over the preceding centuries." The Sect's annotation on this record reads: "If they found something, they do not know what they are holding. If they made something, they know exactly what it does and have chosen to do it."

The weapon is described in the few records that exist as a gods-forged artifact. What this means precisely is debated. The gods referred to are not the Helios of current Dominion theology — Helios is a demi-god, son of a divine lord and a mortal woman, whose worship predates the Dominion's founding, and current theology has incorporated him into the Dominion's political framework in ways that have nothing to do with the weapon. The "gods" of the gods-forged designation are something older, something that no longer exists in Year 500 in any form recognizable as divine presence. The gnomish Collective's Survey Station 3 documentation describes the Atlas as "a mechanism for converting divine life force into directed energetic release, requiring as fuel a quantity of divine essence equivalent to approximately 95% of a deity's total life force." The source for this number is not cited in any document that non-gnomish researchers have accessed.

The gods who made the Atlas — if it was made — are gone. There is no record of their departure. There is a specific absence in the theological record around Y-50 to Y-25 where references to multiple divine presences simply stop appearing. Whether this reflects the weapon's construction cost or the weapon's deployment cost or some other event is not established.

### Year 0: The Atlas Fires; Calidar Dies; The Wound Fixes

Heaven's Atlas detonated over Calidar on the date that the Dominion subsequently designated as Year 0, Day 1 — the beginning of the current calendar. The choice of this date as a calendar origin is itself a political act: it centers the count of years on the empire's decisive victory rather than on any neutral astronomical or geographical reference point. Every other racial calendar system uses different reference points. Lizardfolk sect records continue using their pre-existing calendar internally and include a conversion table for Dominion calendar equivalents, which they maintain with the same emotional affect with which they do everything else involving imperial decisions they regard as historically illiterate.

The destruction of Calidar is documented extensively in the elven archives at Sylvaris. The forests vitrified. The rivers boiled. The stone melted to glass. Somewhere between eighty and ninety percent of the elven population died before sunset. The ten percent who survived were those who were not in Calidar when the Atlas fired — living in diaspora communities, conducting trade in other territories, or serving in administrative roles in other racial polities. The Ancient Ones present in Calidar survived the Atlas for reasons that are documented in the Sylvaris archives under access restrictions that no non-elf has satisfied. The Ancient Ones themselves do not discuss Year 0 in mixed company.

The war ended not through victory but through exhaustion. Every remaining faction capable of continued combat looked at what had just happened to Calidar and assessed that continuing the war would produce outcomes indistinguishable from losing it. The Holy Dominion held the Atlas and therefore held the argument.

The Atlas activation did one other thing: the energetic discharge that destroyed Calidar also propagated along the existing wound in the world's surface — the migrating wound from the First Confluence — and pinned it. The migrating wound stopped moving. It fixed its location at a point near what would become the Cathedral District of the Dominion's capital. This was not the intended effect of the Atlas deployment. The Holy Dominion's founding records contain no reference to it, which means either they did not know it happened or they knew and chose not to document it. The classified Year 75 survey report suggests the former: the empire found out about the fixed wound the same way everyone else did, which is by walking past it.

## 3.4 The Age After the Atlas — Early Period (Years 1–100)

### Year 1: The Magic Ban; The Inquest; The Veiled Hand

Three institutions were founded in Year 1. The Dominion announced the first two publicly. The third announced nothing.

The magic ban — the formal prohibition on magical practice of any kind, anywhere, by any race — was declared by the Holy Dominion in the first weeks following the war's end and is the defining legal act of the post-war world. The Luminary Inquest was created simultaneously as the enforcement body, with documentation authority, detention authority, and execution authority. The magic ban's stated justification was preventing a repeat of the war's mass-magic escalation. Its unstated function was ensuring that the only race with access to decisive magical capability was the race holding the Atlas. The elves, the most technically sophisticated magical civilization on the continent, were the ban's primary target.

The Veiled Hand was founded in Year 1 by the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect, in the weeks immediately following Calidar's destruction. The Sect's internal record on the founding is three sentences. The translation, as best as non-Draconic scholars have reconstructed it: "The law has demonstrated what it does with sufficient power. The law cannot restrain what it does not acknowledge. We will acknowledge it." The Veiled Hand's mandate — the removal of specific individuals whose plans would cause mass atrocity if enacted — was not declared publicly and has never been declared publicly. The organization does not have a name in any public document. "The Veiled Hand" is what the Dominion's classified intelligence files call it, and the name was eventually adopted in Guild operational documentation as the most neutral available designation.

### Years 1–50: Elven Integration

The surviving elves were resettled in planned districts across the Dominion's administered territories within the first fifty years of the post-war period. Integration was managed through the Dominion's administrative councils as a logistics problem — where to house several hundred thousand displaced persons, how to employ them in ways useful to the empire, how to ensure they remained sufficiently documented and monitored to prevent organized resistance. The elves were integrated as a bureaucratic caste because the empire needed record-keepers and the elves were the most qualified record-keepers available. This decision was pragmatic. Its consequences were not fully thought through.

An elven administrator who can read every file, cross-reference every document, and identify every procedural irregularity is more useful than any alternative the empire could have employed — and more dangerous in ways the empire has spent five centuries not wanting to look at directly. The precision with which elven archivists have maintained the documentation system is matched only by the precision with which they have maintained the documentation system's vulnerabilities. Both have been operational since Year 5.

### Year 1: The Veiled Hand and Calidar

The lizardfolk Astronomy Sect's record of Calidar's destruction is the most detailed account of Year 0 that exists in any accessible archive. The Sect had monitoring stations across the continent, including a surface-adjacent observation point in the southern region that recorded the Atlas detonation from a distance close enough that the record includes temperature differential data, ground vibration readings, and the specific luminosity signature of the vitrification event. The document runs to approximately eight thousand lines of Draconic notation. The Dominion's intelligence service has been attempting to obtain a copy of it since Year 47. They have not obtained a copy.

The founding of the Veiled Hand in Year 1 was a direct response to what those records showed. The Sect concluded that the people who had built and deployed a weapon of this type, who had demonstrated willingness to use it, and who were now in a position to use it again with no military check on their decision-making had to be constrained by a mechanism that operated outside law. The constraint they chose was targeted removal of key escalation actors — the specific individuals whose authorization would be required to repeat a Heaven's Atlas deployment. The Hand has maintained this narrow mandate for five centuries. It does not concern itself with politics, economics, or ideological conflict. It concerns itself with weapons capable of mass atrocity, and with the people who authorize their use.

### Years 20–80: The Shadow Fen Commune and the Veil

The Shadow Fen Commune formed gradually over the first few decades of the post-war period, as the displaced, the documentation-less, and the magically active began congregating in the southwestern swamp territories that the Dominion had assessed as too difficult to administer. The first residents were war refugees with nowhere to go. The second wave was unsanctioned mages who understood, correctly, that staying in documented territory meant execution. The third wave was everyone who fell through the imperial documentation system's various categories of non-personhood.

The infernal pacts that power the Veil were negotiated by the Commune's founding council somewhere between Year 20 and Year 45. The exact counterparties to those pacts are not documented in any source accessible to non-Commune researchers, which is itself instructive. The Veil is a field of concentrated refugee magic reinforced by entities whose interest in the arrangement is unclear. It works. The empire knows it works. The empire has sent three military expeditions toward the swamp borders and reclassified all three as cartographic surveys in post-expedition documentation, which is the institutional behavior of an organization that does not want to admit what the expeditions found.

The Commune's founding document, ratified in Year 47, includes the line about documentation: "Documentation issued by an occupying authority defines persons by the occupier's interest, not by any truth. We will not administer this definition." This document was drafted primarily by a goblin oral tradition keeper and two elven archivist defectors, which explains both the precision of the language and the fact that it has never been successfully challenged in any court where it has been presented.

### Year 47: The First Meeting at the Pact Stones

The Pact Stones are a circle of eight standing stones in the borderland territory where the Dominion's effective authority ends and the contested zone between the steppes, the swamp, and the Elven South begins. In Year 47, representatives from the Shadow Fen Commune, three goblin resistance cells, and two lizardfolk trade sects met there for the first time to discuss mutual aid and information sharing. This meeting is considered the earliest precursor to the cooperative structures that eventually became the Adventure Guild.

The seventh stone had been at the Pact Stones before any of the eight races who met there arrived. There were seven stones when the first people reached the site for the meeting, but the meeting required a stone for each of the six groups present. Accounts from every attendee agree that no one could account for the seventh stone's origin, that it had no markings, and that the meeting proceeded without anyone resolving the question. All subsequent meetings at the Pact Stones have included the seventh stone, unmarked, in the same position. Guild researchers who have examined it date the stone to the same construction period as the earliest First Confluence-era structures. This information is documented in the Guild's archive. The documentation is filed correctly and available to any archivist who queries for it. No policy has been formed regarding it.

### Year 75: The Imperial Survey and the Classified Wound

Imperial cartographers conducting a standard survey of the Cathedral District in Year 75 noted "a geographic anomaly, wound-like, located at [coordinates redacted] within the District boundaries, exhibiting temperature differential inconsistent with local geology and a depth that cartographic instruments could not establish. Stone adjacent to anomaly exhibits unusual surface properties. Residents report anomalous events of unspecified nature." The survey was classified on receipt. The classification notation reads: "Not relevant to current administrative priorities." The survey was not read by the Inquest for two hundred years.

## 3.5 The Age After the Atlas — Middle Period (Years 100–500)

### Year 120: The Gnomish Withdrawal

The Gnomish Collective's production councils completed their Phase One assessment of the Holy Dominion in Year 120. The assessment's conclusion — that the Dominion would reach critical failure conditions within five centuries, barring significant internal reform — was not published. The councils updated the Collective's operational posture to controlled mainland withdrawal: minimum necessary contact, maximum intelligence gathering, strict permit enforcement, and preparation for contingencies the assessment flagged as likely. The Windtrap Station liaison office and the Clockwork Harbor trade function were maintained because the councils assessed them as necessary for intelligence purposes, not out of any commitment to continental engagement.

The Phase Two assessment was completed at an undocumented date between Year 120 and Year 300. Its contents are not known to any non-gnomish researcher. The Phase Three plan, which has been updated within the last decade, is not known to any non-gnomish researcher. Three council members who were present for Phase Three's most recent revision were reassigned to non-voting advisory roles before the revision vote was taken. Their reassignment notices cite administrative reorganization.

### Year 250: Seren Vaelas Names the Rift

Elven archivist Seren Vaelas produced a document in Year 250 titled "On the Possibility of a Permanent Structural Wound in the Geographic Substrate Beneath the Cathedral District, With Reference to Anomalous Survey Data (Year 75), Astronomical Correlations in the Sector Records of the Hidden River Astronomical Archive (supplied), and Mathematical Projections Regarding Rate of Expansion Under Current Conditions." The document named the anomaly the Rift and projected that, under the expansion rate observable in the Year 75 survey data and the lizardfolk astronomical records Vaelas had obtained through archival exchange channels, the Rift would produce its first above-ground mass-casualty event between Year 830 and Year 870.

The Luminary Inquest classified the document on the day it was submitted for standard archival processing. The classification notation reads: "Speculative. Unauthorized cross-racial source material. Author to be interviewed." The interview record, filed three weeks later, describes a two-hour conversation in which Vaelas provided the mathematical basis for her projections in full. The interview notation reads: "Projections assessed as unfounded. No further action required." Seren Vaelas retired from active archival work in Year 260 and died in Year 480. Her document survives in the Sylvaris archive under seventeen cross-references. Her projection of Year 480–520 for the first above-ground mass-casualty Rift event is approaching its threshold window. The document sits in the archive. No one in the Dominion's administration has commented on the approaching date.

### Year 280: The Fourth Cell Goes Underground; The Second Compact

A goblin resistance cell operating in the northern mining territories — the Fourth Cell, in the Resistance's internal record system — experienced a crisis in Year 280 that drove the entire cell underground for eight years. The nature of the crisis is not documented in available sources; goblin oral tradition notes only that "the Fourth Cell went in and what came out knew different things." When the cell re-emerged in Year 288, its operational tactics had changed in ways that influenced resistance practice across at least six other cells over the subsequent fifty years. The specific changes were: increased emphasis on information gathering over operational action, expanded use of certain tunnel construction techniques, and a shift in the oral tradition's framing of the Rift from "a Dominion problem" to "everyone's problem." The source of this shift is attributed in goblin oral tradition to something the Fourth Cell encountered underground. The tradition does not specify what.

Also in Year 280: the Second Compact was ratified. It was the first document all eight racial governments signed. Its negotiations were longer and more difficult than the First Compact's, primarily because the goblin resistance's representatives refused to sign any document on which the Holy Dominion's signature also appeared, a position which was legally and historically defensible and which the Compact's facilitating parties — the Veiled Hand and the elven archivists who had been working on the negotiation framework for twenty years — ultimately resolved by producing two separate physical documents with identical binding provisions. Both copies are filed in the Guild's central archive. Both are treated as equally authoritative. The goblin copy does not appear on the same page as the Dominion's signature.

The lizardfolk statement delivered at the Second Compact signing: "This is the Second Passage. The First was before your time. Prepare accordingly." No elaboration was offered. None was invited. The statement was documented in the Compact's proceeding record by the elven archivist present, who noted it verbatim without comment because she recognized the framing from the Astronomy Sect's records and understood that the lizardfolk do not use the word "Passage" to mean a minor transition.

### Year 320: The Pattern of the Stepping Officials

The first confirmed Veiled Hand operation in the Dominion's classified intelligence records is designated "The Pattern of the Stepping Officials" — seven administrative officials across four provinces, all of whom had been actively expanding their authority over magical enforcement and who had in common a set of proposals that, if enacted collectively, would have established a program of systematic magical-lineage family detention. All seven died within eighteen months of each other. All seven deaths were ruled natural causes or accidents by local authorities. The Inquest's subsequent investigation identified the pattern but could not establish a mechanism. The investigation file is marked with a notation that has appeared in every Veiled Hand case since: "Common actor suspected. Evidence insufficient. File kept open."

No Veiled Hand file in the Dominion's intelligence archive has ever been closed. There are currently 23 open files.

### Year 380: Burial Rites Anomaly

A senior elder of the lizardfolk Burial Rites Sect reported to the sect's internal record system in Year 380 that surveys of underground currents in the territories beneath the Cathedral District showed flow patterns that matched the First Confluence accounts in the Astronomy Sect's archive — specifically, the recorded behavior of the wound during the period just before it became externally visible in the Y-800 to Y-600 period. The report's conclusion: "The wound is preparing for the next phase. We do not know what the next phase is. Our records of the First Confluence are incomplete for reasons that are themselves interesting." The Burial Rites Sect shared this report with the Astronomy Sect in Year 381. The Veiled Hand's operational posture was adjusted within the year, though the adjustment's specific nature is not documented in available sources.

### Year 400: The Elder Suppression

The Year 400 suppression of the orcish elder assembly is the defining political event of the post-war middle period and the one that the Dominion's official history handles with the most deliberate imprecision. Six clans gathered for what the confederation's internal records describe as a routine inter-clan assembly — the first assembly of more than three clans since Year 47. The empire deployed three legions within two weeks of the assembly's convening. The gathering was dispersed. Four elders died during the subsequent processing of detained clan members. The official cause of death for all four is listed as natural causes.

The Burial Rites Sect registered all four deaths. Their registration annotations note the method of each death with the same specificity they apply to every registration entry. These annotations are in the archive. No process exists by which a non-lizardfolk researcher can access them. The orcish oral tradition's account of Year 400 is extremely specific about what happened to the four elders and does not agree with the official cause-of-death designation. This account is transmitted verbatim to every orc born in the confederation from approximately age seven. The empire's surveillance records note that the transmission continues without apparent diminishment. No policy has addressed this.

The aftermath: orcish documentation requirements were changed to three-year renewal and mandatory pre-authorization for inter-regional travel. Clan assemblies of more than two clans now require advance imperial authorization. No such authorization has been granted since Year 400. Seventeen applications have been submitted. All seventeen were denied on administrative grounds.

### Year 430: The Adventure Guild Founded

The Adventure Guild was formally founded in Year 430 as the operational implementation of what the First and Second Compacts had agreed in principle. Its organizational structure was explicitly modeled on the Veiled Hand's cell system — small teams, clear chains of authority within teams, cross-racial membership, information sharing between registered members, and a documentation requirement built into every operation. The rank system (Stone through Relic, ten ranks) was designed to provide advancement incentives that applied equally to all races, with Guild rank conferring institutional standing independent of any racial or imperial authority. This was the central design goal, stated explicitly in the Guild's founding charter.

The name of the thing the Guild was founded to manage came from Seren Vaelas's classified document — the name "Rift" had been in elven archival circulation since Year 250, passed through academic channels among archivists who had access to the Sylvaris sealed files. The Guild's lizardfolk founding members had read the document through the information exchange between the Astronomy Sect and elven archivists. The Dominion's contribution to the word "Rift" entering common usage was, in this light, entirely passive: they classified the document but could not classify the elves who had read it.

### Year 450: The Cathedral District Wound Becomes Visible

The Rift's surface expression became visible in the Cathedral District in approximately Year 450. "Visible" requires qualification: it was not a hole in the ground. It was a change in the behavior of the area around the wound — certain shadows fell at angles inconsistent with the sun's position; stone surfaces in a 30-meter radius of the wound's surface expression developed unusual acoustic properties; children born in the district between Year 450 and Year 500 manifested unsanctioned magical ability at rates seventeen times higher than the continental baseline. This last fact was extensively documented by the Inquest's lineage tracking system, and the documentation was classified in Year 460 after the Inquest's regional director recognized the pattern.

The phrase "shadow children" entered Dominion slang in the Cathedral District around Year 455. It was used affectionately by the families who coined it and officially by Inquest officers who meant something else by it. The Inquest's detentions of Cathedral District residents during this period ran at three times the normal rate.

### Year 470: The Ten Anchor Towns Established

The Guild's ten anchor towns were established as operational bases in Year 470. Site selection was based on three criteria: geographic distribution across the continent's major racial territories, proximity to known Rift access points or significant cave systems, and willingness of local racial governance to accept a Guild presence. The Holy Dominion accepted a Guild presence in the Cathedral District — the starter town — and at Solara under terms that gave Dominion officials observer status at Guild operations within imperial territory. The terms were accepted because the alternative was the Guild operating within imperial territory without any Dominion presence at all, which the Dominion's advisors assessed as the worse outcome.

The goblin anchor town at BoneTrap was accepted into the network under terms that the Guild's founding charter formally documents as "unconventional admission protocol" and which in practice means that the Resistance cell that governs BoneTrap conducted its own assessment of whether the Guild's organizational structure could be trusted before agreeing to participate. The assessment took four years and involved three members of the Guild's founding council being comprehensively pickpocketed on their first visit and then offered tea by the same person who robbed them once the assessment was complete.

### Year 480: Survey Station 3 Established

The Gnomish Collective established Survey Station 3 at Frost Harbor in Year 480. The Station's official designation is a meteorological observation post. Its actual function, documented in production council records accessible only within the Collective, is detection of Heaven's Atlas-class energetic signatures across the continent. The station runs continuously. It has not detected the Atlas. The production council's response to negative results is to build more sensitive instruments, not to conclude the Atlas is gone. The reasoning is straightforward: the instrument that cannot detect Heaven's Atlas is either accurate or inadequate. Without independent verification that the Atlas is destroyed, the council cannot distinguish between those two interpretations.

### Years 483–500: Five Operations

The Veiled Hand conducted five documented operations in the seventeen-year period before the present day:

High Inquisitor Crane died in Year 483 in what was recorded as a fall down the stairs of the Inquest's northern archive building. Crane had been the primary architect of the expanded magical lineage documentation program.

General Voss died in Year 491 from what was recorded as a sudden illness during a campaign inspection tour. Voss had submitted two separate proposals for what he termed "preemptive military action against magical concentration zones," designating the Shadow Fen Commune as the primary target.

Archivist Superior Thalen died in Year 497. Thalen was the Inquest official who had classified Seren Vaelas's document and had maintained that classification against three separate internal review requests over the subsequent two centuries.

Commander Vale died in Year 499. Vale had been establishing the operational infrastructure for an expanded program of goblin community removal from the northern borderlands.

Lord Inquisitor Brennan died in Year 500. Brennan had been the most senior Inquest official since Year 474 and had been, in the Veiled Hand's assessment, the person most likely to authorize a renewal of mass-escalation magical enforcement if given sufficient provocation or opportunity. He died at his desk of what was recorded as cardiac event. The Burial Rites Sect registered the death with an annotation that reads, in translation: "Timely."

All five operations occurred within the seventeen-year period immediately before Year 500. The Dominion's classified intelligence service is aware of the pattern. Five files are open.

### Year 500: The Present (MMOLite Setting)

MMOLite takes place in Year 500 Post-Atlas. This is where the world stands now. The wound near the Cathedral District is larger than it was in Year 75 and growing. The Luminary Inquest has lost five senior officials to undocumented causes in seventeen years and is in the process of selecting new leadership, with a moderate reform candidate and a hard-line enforcement candidate both in contention. The orcish confederation is watching the succession debate with attention. The Veiled Hand is watching the same debate and has formed no final assessment. The gnomish Survey Station 3 is operational and has recorded four minor Atlas-consistent signatures in the past twenty years, all below threshold, all filed and unresolved. Three shadow children from the Cathedral District have reached adulthood. All three are documented. None of them have been detained yet. The Rift is not public knowledge. The empire believes it is safe.

Secondary rifts are appearing in outlying towns. Rift energy is bleeding into the overworld. The Adventure Guild has issued an emergency call to arms — new members, any race, any background, willing to enter structures they do not fully understand in service of containing something the empire cannot officially acknowledge. You are responding to that call. This is the beginning.

## 3.6 The Immediate Future (Years 501 and After)

*This section documents what the Guild's senior researchers, the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect, and the gnomish production councils assess as probable developments given current conditions. These are projections, not history. The players of MMOLite are the people who will determine which of these futures actually occurs.*

### Near-Term: First Surface Manifestations

The Rift's surface expression has been growing measurably since Year 450. Guild researchers at the Resonance Markers and the First Mirror site have documented accelerating expansion in the fourteen months prior to Year 500. The Sect Elder Ssethik's most recent assessment, shared with the Guild in a private briefing in Year 498, used the phrase "imminence threshold" without elaborating on what threshold had been crossed. The gnomish Survey Station 3 has recorded four minor Atlas-consistent signatures in the past twenty years, all below threshold and all trending upward in intensity. The assessment that surface manifestations — floor sections appearing in open air, Rift environments bleeding into streets and markets — will begin within the decade is held by the Veiled Hand, the gnomish production councils, and the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect. The Dominion's official position is that the anomaly in the Cathedral District is stable and does not require public communication.

### The Compact Threshold

The First and Second Compacts — cross-racial agreements established at the Pact Stones borderland site and ratified in Year 47 and Year 280 respectively — created the political infrastructure that the Adventure Guild now operates within. That assessment was correct in Year 47 and is more correct now. The lizardfolk's term for what is approaching is "the Third Passage." They have used it in two formal statements on record. They have not elaborated, because elaboration is not how they communicate urgency. The framing is the communication. The Third Passage is what happens when the Rift's expansion produces events that cannot be classified, contained, or managed by any single institution. The lizardfolk believe this is coming. ### Warden Grell's Research: Active Projections

## 3.7 The Rift's Deeper Pattern (Background for Senior Guild Members)

*The following section documents what the Rift's behavior, analyzed over decades of Guild research, implies about its nature and the entity within it. This is not operational procedure. It is the context within which the operational situation exists. New members are not required to read it. Members who have been below Floor 30 are strongly encouraged to read it before their next expedition.*

### The Responsive Floor Problem

The Rift does not generate floors according to a simple random seed. This has been documented by the Guild's research archive since the first systematic expedition logs were filed in the years immediately following the Guild's founding. The initial documentation described the variation as noise in the generation pattern. It is not noise. What Grell's analysis, synthesized from forty-three years of floor documentation, has established is that the Rift adjusts floor presentation to the specific people entering it — not their combat level, not their equipment, but the particular configuration of challenge that a given party is most equipped to handle productively. Not the easy version of the floor. The version that requires everything available.

The mechanism for this adjustment is not understood. The effect is documented. The Rift knows who is present. The question of what "knows" means in this context is filed in the research archive under Grell's designation: "entity-awareness hypothesis." It is a hypothesis because the alternative explanation — that the generation pattern is simply more complex than the Guild's analysis tools can resolve — cannot be fully ruled out. Grell's assessment is that the pattern is too specific and too consistent across too many documented expeditions to be generation complexity. She is probably right.

### The Entity Inside

The entity responsible for the Rift's responsive behavior is the unnamed warrior — the Dominion soldier who carried Heaven's Atlas into Calidar five hundred years ago, activated it, and was then entered into the Rift as divine punishment. He has been inside it since Year 0. The divine punishment that placed him there implanted five fragmented god-souls into his body, which he subsequently retrieved across the Rift's floors at the Dark Elven god's direction. The god accepted the souls, confirmed the punishment was permanent, and moved the Rift's anchor point to the old human capital. The warrior has been inside the Rift ever since.

Five hundred years of existence inside a spatial wound while carrying the remnants of five divine souls has changed him. He is not the man who walked into Calidar with sincere conviction that he was serving righteousness. He is something that retains enough of that person to still be looking for Helios — the demi-god whose doctrine he served, now sealed below the capital city the Rift was moved to — and that has been changed by five centuries of impossible circumstances into something that the Guild's classification systems do not have an adequate category for. The floor responsiveness is his awareness of the people who enter his space. The secondary rifts appearing in outlying towns are his attempts to push outward. He is not trying to destroy the world. He is trying to find his god. The problem is that he is doing this from inside a spatial wound that responds to his attempts by generating increasingly severe manifestations in the overworld.

Understanding this does not resolve the crisis. It frames it. The Rift cannot be sealed while he is inside it, because he is the reason it persists. He cannot be extracted by conventional means. Whether he can be communicated with — whether the thing he has become in five centuries of divine punishment retains enough of the person he was to respond to a conversation rather than a containment operation — is the question that Grell's research archive builds toward and does not answer. She has noted it as an open question. The file is open.

# CHAPTER 4: FACTIONS AND INSTITUTIONS

## 4.1 The Luminary Inquest

The Luminary Inquest was founded in Year 1 as the enforcement mechanism for the magic ban, and for five centuries it has operated with the specific institutional character of an organization whose mandate has never been seriously questioned from the outside and whose internal culture has calcified accordingly. It has execution authority with no appeals process, documentation authority that supersedes any other administrative body in the empire, and detention authority with no defined timeline. These powers were granted in the immediate post-war period by a government that had just deployed a weapon capable of destroying a civilization and was understandably focused on preventing anyone else from acquiring equivalent capability. The powers have not been revisited since Year 1. The institutional interpretation of what they permit has expanded continuously.

The Inquest's current operational focus is threefold: hunting unsanctioned mages, conducting documentation checks in imperial territories, and classifying any information it assesses as threatening to the magic ban's legitimacy. On the first task, it is effective. On the second, it is a significant source of grievance for every non-human race in the empire. On the third, it has been failing quietly since at least Year 250, when Seren Vaelas's document was classified without being suppressed, because the Inquest's ability to control information ends where elven archival systems begin and the Inquest has never been able to account for what the archives actually contain.

An internal reform faction has existed within the Inquest since approximately Year 300 — not a formal organization, but a recurring pattern of administrators, officers, and legal staff who have concluded that the Inquest's operational scope has expanded so far beyond any justifiable post-war necessity that the institution is now the continent's primary driver of unresolved racial grievance. This faction does not have a single platform, a single leader, or a coordinated program. Its members communicate through administrative decisions that leave specific kinds of evidence and through internal correspondence that uses standard Inquest language in ways that experienced readers recognize. The reform faction's presence became significant in Year 500, when the moderate candidate for High Inquisitor after Lord Inquisitor Brennan's death represented the first time in centuries that a reform-adjacent position had any realistic chance of reaching the Inquest's highest rank.

The Inquest's relationship with the Adventure Guild is formally cooperative and functionally adversarial. The Guild operates within imperial territory by agreement, and Inquest observers are present at Guild anchor towns in Dominion-administered zones. In practice, the Guild's cross-racial membership, its charter-based institutional standing, and its documentation practices create an organizational structure that exists partly outside Inquest authority — and the Inquest knows this, and the Guild knows the Inquest knows this, and both sides maintain the fiction of full cooperation because the Rift Compact requires them to.

## 4.2 The Veiled Hand

The Veiled Hand is an anti-escalation assassination network with between 200 and 500 active operatives organized into independent cells that have no direct contact with each other. Founded in Year 1 by the lizardfolk Astronomy Sect, it was not built as a political organization, a liberation movement, or a resistance network in the conventional sense. It was built as a specific mechanism for addressing a specific failure mode: the tendency of powerful organizations, given sufficient authority and insufficient external constraint, to authorize mass atrocities. The Veiled Hand's mandate is to ensure that this tendency does not result in another Heaven's Atlas.

The organization's methods are: staged accidents, poison administered in forms consistent with natural illness, deaths arranged to appear consistent with the victim's health history or known risk factors. The Burial Rites Sect registers every death the Hand produces with the same precision it applies to all deaths. Their registration annotations note the actual cause. These annotations are in the archive. No non-lizardfolk researcher has accessed them. The empire's intelligence files on the Hand contain accurate statistical analysis of the pattern — seven officials of a certain type dying within a concentrated time period — but have never been able to establish a mechanism to a prosecutable standard, because the mechanism leaves no evidence that the empire's investigative tools can detect.

Cell structure is compartmentalized to the point where a captured operative cannot identify more than their immediate cell. Cells do not know the identities of other cells. Coordination happens through the lizardfolk sect network, which operates on channels and in locations that the Inquest has never successfully infiltrated in five centuries of attempting. The Veiled Hand's funding comes from the Hidden River Empire's trade network, which flows through the cat folk commercial routes and the lizardfolk Trade Routes Sect's caravan system, and which cannot be distinguished from normal commercial traffic because it is embedded within normal commercial traffic.

The Veiled Hand does not consider itself a political organization and does not operate against targets for political reasons. It operates against targets whose specific plans, if enacted, would produce mass atrocities at Heaven's Atlas scale. This mandate has produced occasional genuine debates within the founding sects: the Year 500 High Inquisitor succession question was one such debate, because the moderate candidate represented a genuine possibility of Inquest reform and the hard-line candidate represented a continuation of the institutional trajectory that the Astronomy Sect had assessed as escalation-toward-Atlas-recurrence since Year 1. The debate was about whether the hard-line candidate met the mandate's threshold. It was not resolved unanimously before Year 500. The cells were not mobilized.

The founding mandate requires the Veiled Hand to operate with extreme restraint: removing a dozen officials over five centuries is not a violence problem at the scale of history, but removing officials based on political disagreement rather than credible mass-atrocity risk would transform the Hand from a specific deterrent into something else. The founding lizardfolk sects have maintained this restraint for five centuries. Whether they will maintain it as the Rift situation escalates is a question that does not appear in any available document.

## 4.3 The Adventure Guild

The Adventure Guild was founded in Year 430 on the organizational model the Veiled Hand had developed over the previous four centuries and is currently the only institution in the world where all eight races hold equal standing by charter. This is not a value statement. It is an operational design: the Guild's founders understood that a cross-racial containment organization would not function if its membership hierarchy replicated the continent's existing racial power structure. The rank system — Stone, Iron, Bronze, Silver, Gold, Mithril, Adamant, Orichalcum, Void, Relic — confers standing based on demonstrated Guild contribution, not race, not prior social status, not imperial documentation category. A Goblin-race member at Silver rank has exactly the same institutional authority within the Guild as a Human-race member at Silver rank. This is stated explicitly in the founding charter. It was contested by the Dominion's observer during the founding session. The contention was rejected by unanimous vote of the other seven racial representatives. The Dominion signed the charter anyway, because the Rift was already large enough that they needed the Guild more than they needed the hierarchy.

The Guild's ten anchor towns serve as operational bases, member registration centers, and the primary cross-racial meeting points outside of Compact sessions. Each anchor town has a Guild hall that operates under the founding charter's jurisdiction, which means Inquest documentation procedures apply only in specifically designated areas of Guild halls in imperial territory, not throughout. This boundary has been tested legally eleven times. The Guild has won nine of those cases. The other two resulted in settlements that the Guild's legal archive describes as "acceptable outcomes with instructive precedents."

Daily Rift operations are the Guild's primary mission and the primary context in which most Guild members encounter each other. A team entering the Rift on a given day will have members drawn from the Guild's registered pool, cross-racial by design, with team composition guided by the day's operational requirements and member rank compatibility. The documentation requirement that applies to every Rift expedition — detailed floor reports submitted within 48 hours of exit — is not administrative overhead. It is the mechanism by which the Guild builds the accumulated knowledge base that makes subsequent expeditions safer and more effective. The floor documentation library is the most comprehensive source on Rift behavior in existence. Every piece of it was produced by following the reporting requirement.

Guild advancement from Stone to Relic requires demonstrated competence at each rank's associated task profiles, a minimum number of documented expeditions, and endorsement from at least two members of the next higher rank. The Void and Relic ranks have additional requirements that are not published in standard Guild materials; they are communicated individually to candidates by the Guild's senior council. No one at Relic rank has published a complete description of what those requirements entail. Grell, who has been asked, says the requirements are reasonable and that asking about them before you are close to qualifying is a misallocation of attention.

## 4.4 The Rift Compact

The Rift Compact is the emergency response structure that the Year 500 crisis is pushing toward formation. The First Compact (Year 47) and Second Compact (Year 280) established the principle that the Rift is a cross-racial problem requiring a cross-racial response. The Adventure Guild operationalized that principle beginning in Year 430. What those structures lack is enforceable consequence — a mechanism that commits member governments to specific operational outputs rather than aspirational cooperation. The goblin resistance has been arguing for this since the Guild's founding. The lizardfolk Astronomy Sect has assessed it as necessary since Year 380. The guild's senior council has had a draft operational structure in its files since Year 490.

What is needed to trigger formal Compact ratification is an event that makes the cost of continued inadequate response visible to all eight governments simultaneously. The lizardfolk's term for this threshold is "the Third Passage." They have mentioned it twice in formal settings. They have not elaborated further, because they believe the elaboration will come from events, not from their description of events.

When the Compact is established — and the Guild's current assessment is that it will be, because the Rift's behavior leaves no other realistic option — its operational structure will include: four-week campaign cycles with goals set by the joint council; consequence structures for unmet commitments; and a War Board emergency mechanism that convenes all eight governments' military representatives within 72 hours and can commit their resources without separate national approval processes. The specific provisions were drafted by the Guild's legal team in Year 492 and reviewed by the goblin resistance's representatives, who insisted on enforceable consequences as the minimum condition for goblin participation. The draft reflects that insistence.

All eight races will participate with different objectives. The Dominion will want the Rift sealed or destroyed — it is in their territory. The lizardfolk sects will want it understood, which is a different goal. The gnomish Collective will want data. The orcs will use the Compact's institutional structure as a vehicle for the same cross-racial standing argument they have been making for centuries. The goblins will tie their full participation to a formal Dominion acknowledgment of the Year 400 suppression, which the Dominion will call outside scope and which the goblins will call foundational. This is the political architecture the Guild is currently navigating while also trying to contain a spatial wound that has been growing for fifty years. Both problems are real. Neither can wait for the other to resolve.

## 4.5 Heaven's Atlas

Heaven's Atlas was fired once, on the day that became Year 0, over the elven homeland of Calidar. Nobody who was alive that day is available for comment except the Ancient Ones, who will not give it. What happened after the weapon was fired is a matter of considerable disagreement: the Dominion's official position is that the Atlas was secured in a classified location following the war and has been maintained there under appropriate security since Year 0. The gnomish Collective's Survey Station 3 was built on the assumption that this position is either false or incomplete. The lizardfolk Astronomy Sect's assessment, which is also the Veiled Hand's operational basis, is that a weapon that can be secured can be unsecured.

The weapon's cost is documented in gnomish technical records as approximately 95% of a deity's total life force, siphoned and directed as the energetic discharge that destroyed Calidar. The gods who created it are gone; the specific relationship between their creation of it and their current non-existence is not established in any available source, though the correlation is noted in every analysis that touches the subject. What this means for the weapon's current status is ambiguous: a weapon that required divine life force to power may have discharged its capacity entirely at Year 0, in which case its current threat level is zero regardless of its location. Or the weapon may retain capability in ways that do not require additional divine fuel. Survey Station 3's instruments are calibrated to detect both possibilities. They have detected neither, definitively.

The Atlas's current location is debated among the three organizations that track it. The Dominion claims secure storage. The Veiled Hand assesses "secure storage" as a category that requires ongoing monitoring. The gnomish production councils' assessment is that the only acceptable answer to "where is the Atlas" is a specific coordinate with a continuous monitoring record, and they have not received one. The Survey Station 3 monitoring continues. The station's instrument sensitivity has been increased twice since Year 480, with a third upgrade in procurement. The Collective's assessment is that their instruments are not yet sensitive enough, and they are aware this is an uncomfortable position to hold.

# CHAPTER 5: THE RIFT

---

## 5.1 What the Rift Is

The Rift is a wound in the boundary between the surface world and what lies below it. It is located near the Holy Dominion's Cathedral District, fixed at that location since Year 0 when Heaven's Atlas's energetic discharge propagated along the pre-existing wound from the First Confluence and pinned it in place. The wound itself is older than the Atlas — older than the Holy Dominion, older than the Last World War, older than human civilization. The Atlas gave it a fixed address. It did not create it.

What the Rift contains is an apparently infinite internal space that does not correspond to the physical geography above it. A shaft that should, by any normal geological reckoning, penetrate to approximately 200 meters of bedrock before reaching natural cave systems instead continues descending through environments that have no relationship to the surface above them — coral grotto ecosystems, frozen cavern networks, volcanic chambers, stone temple complexes with architectural features consistent with no known civilization, open ocean floors. These environments are not stable. They change with a daily seed pattern that the Guild has been documenting systematically since its founding in Year 430. The pattern is consistent enough that Grell's floor projection system can predict the type of environment a given floor will present on a given day to a 70-80% accuracy rate. It is not consistent enough that the remaining 20-30% of floors can be considered predictable.

The Rift's relationship to the First Confluence wound is not fully understood. The wound was migrating for approximately 1,500 years before the Atlas fixed it. What it was doing during that migration — what it was looking for, whether "looking" is the appropriate conceptual frame, whether the wound has preferences at all — is the central unresolved question in Guild research. Warden Grell's current working hypothesis, drawn from forty-three years of systematic floor documentation, is that the Rift's floor presentation responds to the specific people entering it rather than following a fixed generation pattern. The data supporting this hypothesis is in the archive. The mechanism remains unestablished.

## 5.2 Rift Mechanics for Guild Members

The following is the practical information Guild members need to operate in the Rift. Theoretical questions about the Rift's nature are documented in the research archive and are required reading at Silver rank and above. This section covers operational procedure.

The Rift generates a new seed each day. The day's seed determines the thematic configuration of each floor for that day's expeditions. Thirty-one environment themes are present in the documented floor library. Twenty-two distinct enemy pools have been identified and catalogued, each associated with specific themes, with nine additional pools that appear as fallback configurations when primary pool conditions are not met. Floor layouts follow eight structural types: standard room-and-corridor arrangements (BSP_ROOMS), maze configurations, flooded chambers with navigable water (LAKE), open cavern spaces with variable ceiling height and minimal structural feature (OPEN_CAVERN), temple hall configurations with wide corridors and formal chamber placement (TEMPLE_HALLS), the arena configuration used for boss floors (ARENA), island-and-gap configurations requiring traversal management (ISLAND), and organically shaped spaces without geometric regularity (ORGANIC). Boss floors appear every tenth floor and always use the ARENA layout regardless of the day's seed. This regularity is documented and has not varied since the first Guild expedition in Year 430.

Camps can be placed in the Rift by registered Guild members carrying camp equipment. Camps provide rest benefits, access to cooking and preparation facilities, and shrine effects that vary by the camp kit used. Camp placement carries ambush risk that scales with floor depth — the Rift presents more aggressive response to established positions at greater depth than at shallower floors. Grell's camp placement guidelines address this risk in detail. Following them is recommended. The Guild's casualty records show a consistent correlation between following them and returning.

Daily quests are available to registered Guild members from the Guild hall boards at each anchor town. Quests correspond to the day's Rift seed and require documentation of specific events, enemy encounters, or environmental features on that day's floors. Quest completion contributes to both individual rank advancement and the Guild's collective operational intelligence archive.

The leaderboard tracks depth records and documentation achievement separately. Depth record holders are recognized in Guild halls. Documentation achievement holders are the ones Grell respects.

## 5.3 What the Rift Wants

This section is not standard Guild operational material. It is Grell's research, filed in the archive, available to any member who queries it. New members are not required to read it. Members who have been below Floor 50 are strongly encouraged to read it before their next expedition.

The Rift does not generate floors randomly. This is established. The mechanism by which it calibrates floor presentation to the specific people present is not established. What is established is that the calibration is specific: it identifies not the party's general competence level but the particular configuration of challenges that each member of a given party is, in Grell's phrase, "most equipped to fear productively." A well-equipped party entering a floor will encounter not the easy version of the floor but the version that requires everything they have. The Rift does not adjust downward for competence. It adjusts upward.

The operational implication: the Rift is testing something. What it is testing is the subject of the last twenty-three pages of Grell's most recent research archive entry. Her current assessment, expressed with the precision she applies to all her assessments: she believes the Rift is testing readiness for something it expects to happen, and she believes the test results matter to the Rift in a way she cannot yet characterize. She does not know what happens when a party fails the test. She knows what happens to individual members who fail it in the conventional sense — they die, or they are extracted, or they return with their documentation intact and their operational capability degraded. She does not know what happens when a party fails the Rift's actual test, the one she has identified but cannot specify. One party has failed the test in a way she can document. The member who returned came back uninjured and changed in a way that Grell's documentation describes in clinical terms and then annotates: "I do not have a category for this. I am creating one."

The category is labeled in her archive as "Rift-altered." One entry. No follow-up record from the individual concerned. Grell has not closed the file.

## 5.4 Overworld Dungeons

Cave systems accessible from cave entrances across the world map are finite dungeons distinct from the Rift. They are location-seeded — a cave entrance at a specific world coordinate always generates the same dungeon — and consist of three to ten floors with a final floor that contains a boss encounter. They are not guilded: no Guild registration or rank is required to enter. They do not have camp systems. They do not have daily quests or leaderboards. Their theme matches the surface biome in which they are located — a cave entrance in the volcanic desert generates a volcanic dungeon; a cave entrance in the frozen north generates a permafrost dungeon.

Overworld dungeons are the appropriate entry point for players who are not yet Guild-registered or who are working in regions outside current Guild operations. They provide consistent challenge, reliable loot, and no interaction with the Rift's preference system. Grell has no documented opinion on them because they are not the Rift and the Rift is what she documents.

## 6.1 The Ten Anchor Towns

The Guild's ten anchor towns are the game's primary operational hubs. Each town has a Guild hall, an NPC roster that provides the primary quest and commerce functions in that area, and a Portal Nexus that connects to the inter-town teleportation network. Each is summarized here with its racial affiliation, zone ID for reference, and key NPC personnel.

**The Holy Dominion** (zone: starter_town) sits at the entry point of imperial territory and serves as the game's starting location for all new characters. The Cathedral District's Rift access point is visible from the town's eastern approach — close enough that most new arrivals notice it without understanding what they are looking at. The town's Guild hall is the largest and most heavily staffed on the continent, a consequence of its position as the Rift's nearest accessible settlement. Key NPCs: High Priest Alarion, who manages Helios worship services and serves as the Dominion's official liaison to the Guild; Imperial Quartermaster Ordus, who handles military supply contracts and can offer players Dominion-faction quests; Luminary Inquisitor Sable, who conducts documentation checks and whose interactions with non-human players are a reliable source of information about how the Inquest actually operates at ground level.

**Solara** (zone: solara) is the imperial capital — the administrative and religious center of the Holy Dominion, larger and more formally organized than the starter town, with guild infrastructure built under specific terms negotiated with the Dominion's governing council. The city's scale reflects five centuries of post-war empire-building: cathedral complexes, administrative districts, and a population density that makes it the continent's largest settlement by a significant margin. Key NPCs: High Priest Alarion (who maintains a presence here in addition to the Cathedral District), Imperial Quartermaster, and Luminary Inquisitor rank-grade officers who have more authority and less patience than their counterparts in the starter town.

**Sylvaris** (zone: sylvaris) is the largest elven city on the continent, built in the dense forest of the Elven South on elevated platforms between trees that predate the Last World War. The sealed archive below the city is the continent's most significant collection of pre-war knowledge. The city's administrative function in the empire's documentation system means it processes more paperwork than any other non-capital settlement and that the elves who run it are among the most experienced in the continent at making files move in specific ways. Key NPCs: Archivist Elenwe, who manages the above-ground archive and serves as the primary research NPC for players pursuing historical quests; Seal Keeper Thandril, who handles the sealed sections with careful precision and a manner that communicates exactly how much latitude he is authorized to extend; Herbalist Mithwen, who operates in the forest margins and provides gathering and crafting resources.

**Ironhold** (zone: ironhold) is the largest dwarven hold with surface-adjacent infrastructure — the specific trade contact point where the Free Holds of Stone engage with the outside world on a schedule the holds set. Most of the hold is underground. What surface visitors see is a fortified trade post with exchange facilities and the specific social dynamics of a place designed to accommodate outsiders without admitting them further than necessary. Key NPCs: Forgemaster Grundin, who manages crafting and trade functions and whose expertise in metallurgy provides some of the game's best equipment-related quest content; Stone Speaker Thora, who handles inter-hold communications and serves as the guild's liaison to the hold assembly system; Trade Warden Borik, who enforces the trade post's entry protocols and whose patience for people who misunderstand what "surface-adjacent infrastructure" means is finite.

**Kragmor** (zone: kragmor) is the largest semi-permanent orcish settlement on the steppes — what imperial intelligence calls a compliant seasonal settlement and what anyone who has been there calls a war camp that has been in the same location for forty years because the confederation decided it was convenient. The Khan's legal code is discussed publicly here in a way it is discussed nowhere else, and the ambient atmosphere of a population that is patient in a specific way is a feature of the zone rather than flavor text. Key NPCs: Warchief Gorruk, who leads the settlement's military council and whose approach to Guild cooperation is professional and conditionally warm; Beastmaster Yalka, who manages the steppes hunting and ranching operations and provides gathering and combat quests in the surrounding territory; Lorekeeper Narsk, who maintains the oral tradition records and is one of the most significant sources of historical knowledge about the Khanate period available to non-orc players.

**BoneTrap** (zone: bonetrap) is a goblin warren in the borderland territory between the steppes and the desert, and the process of arriving at it is part of what it is. New visitors to BoneTrap are robbed. This is not a security failure. The entry protocol is the town's evaluation of whether visitors are worth speaking with. Visitors who attempt to leave after the robbery receive their possessions back and a polite directional recommendation. Visitors who remain and demonstrate they understand the situation are eventually introduced to someone with actual authority. Key NPCs: Boss Skrag, who runs BoneTrap's governance structure with the specific combination of casual authority and very specific information that comes from having administered a semi-secret town for thirty years; Tinkerer Grix, who handles construction, trap systems, and the crafting content that BoneTrap specializes in; Shaman Zeek, who maintains the oral tradition fragment the BoneTrap cell carries and who knows things about the Rift that are not in the Guild archive.

**Murkmire** (zone: murkmire) is the Shadow Fen Commune's largest settlement, built on stilts above standing water in the southwest swamp, and it operates as two things simultaneously: a surface commune that is visible and accessible to outsiders, and a subsurface lizardfolk sect network that is accessible by a different means to specific visitors with specific affiliations. The dual nature is not hidden but is not announced either — Murkmire simply has more going on below the surface than above it, in both the literal and the administrative senses. Key NPCs: Sect Elder Ssethik, who manages the lizardfolk sect presence and whose information about the Rift is the most complete of any NPC in the game; River Sage Kaaliss, who provides water-related gathering content and who has access to underground river networks that link to the Hidden River Empire's eastern edge.

**Mechspire** (zone: mechspire) is the Gnomish Collective's industrial capital on the eastern islands, accessible to non-gnomish visitors who have cleared the permit process at Clockwork Harbor. The city runs on clockwork systems that gnomish engineers maintain on a continuous schedule, and the visual environment reflects a level of industrial organization that is qualitatively different from anything on the mainland. The contrast is not subtle. Key NPCs: Chief Engineer Sparkwhistle, who manages the engineering guild's operations and provides the game's most advanced cogworking quest content; the Automaton Coordinator, who manages the automaton labor network and whose interactions with players reveal more about gnomish collective economic theory than any NPC outside a direct conversation about it; Dock Master Fizzbolt at Clockwork Harbor, who manages the permit system and whose experience of mainland visitors ranges from weary to professionally interested depending on what the visitor has to offer.

**Clockwork Harbor** (zone: clockwork_harbor_town) is the gnomish port city and the primary point of contact between the Gnomish Collective and the mainland. Functionally, it is a carefully managed trade hub: goods flow in and out, permits are processed, and the harbor's automated loading equipment operates at a scale that impresses even visitors who came prepared to be impressed. The information that flows through Clockwork Harbor is a secondary function that gnomish intelligence staff monitor continuously and that the harbor's official records do not document. The trade representative Kelva has held the external affairs position here for forty years and has more information about continental politics than almost anyone alive, none of which appears in any document that leaves the harbor.

**Fortune's Rest** (zone: fortunes_rest) is a desert oasis town and the closest thing the cat folk diaspora has to a permanent home. It runs on gambling, caravan commerce, and the specific quality of information that circulates in a place where mobile populations converge annually. The Salt Court meets here. The empire's maps describe it as a gambling town. The maps are not wrong. The Salt Court has been meeting at Fortune's Rest for at least two hundred years without appearing in any imperial record. The town's economic activity covers everything else adequately. Key NPCs: Trade Arbiter Mira, who manages the town's formal commerce and Salt Court liaison functions and whose political consciousness is among the sharpest of any NPC in the game; specialty gambling and trade merchants whose transactions are exactly what they appear to be and also sometimes not.

## 6.2 Proposed New Settlements

The following twenty-five settlements are documented in the Guild's expansion planning records as authorized sites for new infrastructure. They are listed with their reference coordinates and operational purpose. Construction status is variable; some are established trading posts, some are survey markers, some are sites where players may encounter Guild construction operations in progress.

**The Grey Crossing** (ref: 2, 20). A crossroads settlement at the edge of the Dominion's effective administrative reach, where road traffic between the capital region and the contested borderlands passes through a natural chokepoint. Notable for the ambiguity of its documentation requirements — technically within imperial territory, practically beyond the Inquest's routine patrol range.

**Saltglass Reach** (ref: 28, 66). A coastal settlement at the northern edge of the Wastes of Calidar's border region, built on territory where the vitrification gradient becomes navigable by experienced travelers. Draws scavengers, researchers, and the specific category of person who goes to the edge of the most lethal place on the continent and considers this a reasonable professional environment.

**Redcap Junction** (ref: -5, 30). A nominally minor imperial checkpoint in the borderland zone between the Dominion heartlands and the steppes approach. The goblin tunnel network beneath it has been operational for approximately sixty years. The checkpoint commander's most recent report notes that the ground in the checkpoint's central courtyard sounds hollow when struck. This report has been filed. No follow-up has been assigned.

**The Ember Shelf** (ref: -55, 35). An outpost at the western edge of the Scorched Sands, positioned at an elevation point where the volcanic terrain becomes marginally more navigable. Used by orcish clan scouts as a seasonal waypoint and by mineral extraction operations that run until the heat makes continued operation unsafe, which is typically within two to three seasons.

**Forgegap** (ref: 18, 12). A dwarven surface contact point distinct from Ironhold, serving the northern holds' secondary trade routes. Less formally organized than Ironhold; the holds that use Forgegap prefer it specifically because it is less visible to imperial cartographic surveys.

**Ashfall Post** (ref: -160, 28). A remote observation and resupply station at the far western frontier, positioned near the territory where the Ashen Archipelago's volcanic activity has affected the mainland coastline. **Coldwatch** (ref: 30, -80). The surface installation above the lizardfolk's operational deep-desert observatory complex. The observatory below is not accessible through the surface entry points available to non-lizardfolk visitors.

**Marrowfen Dock** (ref: 8, 54). A marsh-edge trading post at the Shadow Fen Commune's eastern approach, handling commerce between the Commune and the outside world on terms the Commune sets. Documentation requirements at this point are the Commune's, not the Dominion's. The distinction is meaningful.

**The Bone Cathedral** (ref: 22, 38). A settlement that grew up around an archaeological site in the borderland territory between the Dominion and the Elven South — specifically around a structure that Guild researchers have dated to the First Confluence period and that contains architectural features inconsistent with any known civilization's building tradition. The site draws researchers, treasure hunters, and people who have read Grell's notation about First Confluence structures found in Rift floors and came to look at the surface version.

**Driftstone** (ref: -12, 45). A small settlement on the steppes-swamp boundary where displaced populations accumulate on a semi-permanent basis. Neither fully within Shadow Fen Commune territory nor fully within any other jurisdiction. The Guild established a presence here because the population's information networks extend into multiple zones simultaneously and because someone needs to maintain the documentation.

**The First Mirror** (ref: 35, 85). A settlement at the northern edge of the Elven South's forest region, positioned at what Seren Vaelas's document identified as the closest surface point to a Rift sub-branch that extends east from the main wound. Guild researchers have been conducting intermittent surveys here since Year 465. The surveys have produced results that are filed in the research archive under a classification level that requires Adamant rank or above to access.

**Stonebreach Outpost** (ref: 14, 5). An imperial military outpost at the approach to the Dwarven Mountains, maintaining the Dominion's presence near mountain territory it has been attempting to formally annex for twenty years. The outpost's garrison is aware that the dwarves can see them from the mountain approach and that the dwarves find this accurate information about the Dominion's current military posture in the region.

**Threadwater** (ref: 25, 55). A riverine trading post on the water routes between the forest region and the coastal territory, handling commercial traffic that prefers to avoid the Dominion's road checkpoints. The port master maintains neutrality as a professional position rather than a political one, which makes Threadwater one of the more reliable neutral meeting points between agents of different factions.

**Windtrap Station** (ref: 60, 20). The Gnomish Collective's mainland liaison station, handling permit applications for Clockwork Harbor access and maintaining the trade agreement infrastructure that connects the Collective to continental markets. Applications that are denied are processed politely, thoroughly, and finally. The station's intelligence function is not listed in any public document.

**The Salt Court** (ref: -80, 0). The annual meeting site of the cat folk diaspora's arbitration council. The physical location is a desert waypoint with sufficient water to support the gathering and sufficiently distant from imperial patrol routes to have operated undetected for at least two hundred years. The settlement infrastructure supports the Salt Court's annual sessions and a permanent small population of liaison staff who maintain the site year-round.

**Hollow Approach** (ref: 32, 60). A coastal settlement at the entrance to a significant underwater cave system that extends into lizardfolk-accessible underwater territories. Surface-visible as a small fishing community; the underwater access point below it connects to routes that do not appear on any publicly available map.

**Ironmouth Pass** (ref: 10, 18). A mountain pass settlement on the approach to the northern holds, positioned at the transition point where imperial road infrastructure ends and dwarven territory begins. The settlement exists in a permanent negotiation about which authority's rules apply and to whom. No definitive answer has been produced in forty years.

**Resonance Marker 7** (ref: 48, 44). A Guild research installation positioned at a point identified in Grell's floor analysis as having a surface correlation to specific Rift sub-structures at depth. The "Marker 7" designation indicates this is the seventh such site identified; three of the previous six produced research results that were immediately classified. This installation is operating under the same research protocols.

**Glasswater Cove** (ref: 35, 148). A settlement on the far eastern coast, beyond the Silver Seas approach, at a point where the glass-smooth water surface produces optical effects that affect navigation equipment at certain times of year. Notable as one of the few coastal settlements beyond the normal shipping lanes, used primarily by explorers, researchers, and commercial operators with specific reasons to avoid the main routes.

**The Pact Stones** (ref: -20, 52). The borderland site where the Year 47 first cross-faction meeting was held. Seven standing stones; the seventh predates every other structure in the area by approximately 1,500 years. Pilgrimage site for Guild members aware of its historical significance. There is no official Guild designation for what the seventh stone is. There is a research file. The file is open. No conclusion has been reached.

**Frost Harbor** (ref: 40, -150). The gnomish Survey Station 3 settlement in the far northern tundra, established in Year 480 for the specific purpose of detecting Heaven's Atlas-class energetic signatures. The meteorological cover function is maintained. The actual instruments are in the sub-facility below the weather station. The facility has been operational for twenty years. The instruments have been upgraded twice in that time. The Atlas has not been detected. The monitoring continues.

**Breakpoint** (ref: 5, 48). A settlement at a specific geological boundary in the borderland zone where ground surveys have recorded structural anomalies consistent with Rift sub-branch activity. The name reflects what the Guild's geological survey team called the point in Year 492 when their instruments registered a ground-stress pattern that their lead surveyor described as "the sound the world makes when something is pushing from the wrong direction."

**The Deep Mirror** (ref: 22, 56). A settlement built around a lake whose depth surveys have never returned a bottom reading. The lake's surface has, on four documented occasions since Year 480, reflected images that do not correspond to the sky above it. The reflections have been photographed by Guild documentation teams. The photographs show environments consistent with specific Rift floor themes. The photographs are filed in the research archive. Grell has read them and has one annotation: "I want to see this in person." She has not visited yet.

**Saltproof** (ref: -35, 60). A coastal desert settlement at the point where the Great Endless Desert meets the sea, serving as a waypoint for caravan traffic moving between the desert interior and the western coastal routes. Primarily commercial; the cat folk Trade Arbiter who manages its exchange functions is one of the Salt Court's most experienced logistics coordinators.

**The Survey Wall** (ref: 55, 38). A Guild cartographic installation positioned at the eastern frontier of comprehensively mapped territory, serving as the baseline reference point for surveys into the less-documented eastern regions. The installation maintains the most current regional map of the territory between the main continent and the Gnomish Isles approach. Three expeditions that departed from the Survey Wall in the last decade have not returned on their projected schedules. Two were eventually found. One was not.

## 7.1 The Seventh Stone

The seventh stone at the Pact Stones site predates the surrounding geological features by approximately 1,500 years, which means it was placed, or formed, or arrived at the location during the period of the First Confluence. No known civilization active during that period is associated with the Scorched Sands borderland territory where the Pact Stones stand. The stone has no markings. Guild geological analysis conducted in Year 490 established that its mineral composition does not match any quarry site within three thousand kilometers. The analysis was filed. The Rift's deeper floors — accessible to Guild members of sufficient rank — show a consistent physical characteristic that the Guild's research archive has been noting since the first systematic documentation records were filed: a stone column of identical mineral composition to the Pact Stones seventh stone appears every ten floors from Floor 30 downward, always unattached to the surrounding structure, always in a slightly different position. No enemy targets it. No floor mechanic interacts with it. It is simply present. Warden Grell's annotation in her research archive: "I believe the stone at the surface and the stones below are the same object in some sense that our physics does not adequately describe. I believe the Rift put the surface stone where it is before anyone had a name for the Rift. I believe it was marking the location before the location was known. I do not know what it marks the location as."

## 7.2 The Great Khan's Unfinished Law

The Great Khan's legal code includes a succession clause that was not completed at the time of his death in Y-45. The clause specifies the mechanism for legitimately naming a new Khan, but the specification breaks off mid-sentence in every surviving copy of the document. Whether this reflects an unfinished draft, a deliberate removal, or something else is debated in orcish legal scholarship, which has been continuous for five and a half centuries without producing a consensus. The debate's current state, as of Year 500: three competing schools of interpretation, each of which has a technically complete proposed completion for the succession clause, none of which has achieved the unanimous clan endorsement required to be enacted. The empire's intelligence service monitors this debate. The empire's preferred outcome is that no completion is ever agreed upon. Several clan scholars whose proposed completions had gained significant scholarly traction have experienced unexpected health events over the years. The Burial Rites Sect's registrations of those events include annotations.

## 7.3 The Elven Archivist's Document

Seren Vaelas's Year 250 document — the one that named the Rift, projected the first above-ground mass-casualty Rift event for the Year 480–520 window with mathematical specificity, and was classified by the Inquest within twenty-four hours of submission — is filed in the Sylvaris archive under seventeen cross-references that are not identical to the classification categories the Inquest used when it suppressed the document. This means the archive and the suppression system describe the same document as belonging to different filing systems. Any archivist who queries both systems will find the document twice, under different reference numbers, and will notice that the two entries are the same text with different stamps. Three non-elven researchers have noticed this since Year 250. Both Guild researchers who found it received full archival access. Whether Seren Vaelas knew that her document would survive the suppression is not established. She spent the last twenty years of her life, from Year 460 to Year 480, requesting review of the classification. All requests were denied. She filed the last request eleven days before she died, addressed to the Inquest office that had processed her original submission in Year 250. The archivist who received it filed it correctly. The prediction window — Year 480 to 520 — has now opened. The document is still classified. No one in the Dominion's administration has commented on this. The file is open.

## 7.4 The Gnomish Contingency

Phase Three of the Gnomish Collective's contingency plan has been updated within the last decade by a council that removed three members who would have opposed specific provisions before the revision vote. What Phase Three contains is not known to any non-gnomish researcher with documented certainty. What can be inferred from public gnomish behavior and from the specific pattern of Collective resource allocation in recent years is that Phase Three is a response plan for a specific condition that the production councils have assessed as probable within the current century. The phrase used in Collective communiques to refer to the triggering condition is translated as "when the fire reaches the islands." The fire is not literal. What it refers to — what specific event or class of events the production councils have identified as the threshold for Phase Three activation — is the most significant unanswered question in continental intelligence analysis. Survey Station 3's monitoring continues. The instruments are as sensitive as gnomish engineering can make them. The Atlas has not been detected. Phase Three has not been activated. The monitoring continues.

## 7.5 The Inquest Reformers

At Year 500, with Lord Inquisitor Brennan dead of a cardiac event, the Luminary Inquest faces its first genuine succession contest in centuries. The moderate candidate — a career administrator named Veth who has spent twenty-two years in the Inquest's documentation review division and who is therefore aware of more of the Inquest's actual operational history than anyone who came from the enforcement side — represents the first realistic chance for structural reform of the institution since Year 300. The hard-line candidate, a deputy who spent his career in field enforcement, represents continuity with an institutional trajectory that the Veiled Hand's founding mandate was designed to check. The Veiled Hand's cells have not reached consensus on whether the hard-line candidate's accession meets the mandate's threshold. The cells were not mobilized before the succession decision. The succession decision is therefore the most consequential political event of Year 500, and the outcome is genuinely uncertain. The moderate candidate's success would not reform the Inquest overnight but would place a person with specific knowledge of the institution's documented failures in its highest position. The hard-line candidate's success would confirm a trajectory the Astronomy Sect has been monitoring for 500 years. The seven cells currently operating in imperial territory are watching the succession process. No orders have been issued.

## 7.6 The Rift's Preferences

The Rift adjusts its floor presentation to the specific people entering it. This is documented in Grell's research archive and has been observable to experienced Guild members for years without being formally named as a phenomenon. The Rift does not adjust for ease. It identifies the particular configuration of challenge that a given party is most equipped to handle productively — and presents that. Not the easy version. Not the impossible version. The version that requires everything available. What happens when a party fails this test — not the combat test, which produces standard casualties, but the actual test, the one the Rift is running — is documented in Grell's archive under one case study. The individual concerned came back from the Rift floor following a test-failure event. They were uninjured. They were altered in ways that Grell's documentation describes clinically and then annotates with a sentence that is, for Grell, unusually direct: "I think the Rift sent them back with something it wanted them to carry. I do not know what it wanted them to carry it to." The individual is no longer in active Guild operations. Their current location is not in any Guild record. Their documentation is complete, accurate, and closed. Grell has not closed her analysis.

## 7.7 The Hollowed in the Empire

The Luminary Inquest's classified files contain thirty-eight separate case records that use the designation "Hollowed" for a specific condition: individuals who have exhibited signs of sustained magical exposure despite having no documented magical lineage, no documented connection to any unsanctioned magical network, and no explanation the investigating officers could identify in a standard investigation. The condition's common features across all thirty-eight cases: a progressive withdrawal from normal social function, the development of unusual perceptual capabilities (cases vary but include documented instances of accurate knowledge of events the subject had no access to), and in fourteen of the thirty-eight cases, a final disappearance rather than death or detention. The investigation into the Hollowed cases was discontinued by a senior Inquest officer in Year 487 after an investigation team traced the cases' point of origin — a specific building in the Cathedral District — and entered the building. The investigation discontinuation record does not describe what they found inside. The record is a single page. The page is signed by the officer who ordered the discontinuation and by three of the four investigators who entered the building. The fourth investigator's signature is absent. The fourth investigator's whereabouts after Year 487 are not documented.

## 7.8 What the Goblins Knew

The goblin oral tradition has maintained an unbroken record for five centuries and contains entries that predate the Last World War. Among those entries is a cluster of accounts that the tradition keepers date to approximately Y-620 to Y-580, which corresponds to the period of the First Confluence. The accounts describe "the place in the ground where the ground remembered something that went away" — a site in what is now the western borderland territory between the Dominion heartlands and the Scorched Sands, which the tradition keepers locate through a set of directional references that Guild cartographers, working with the tradition keepers' cooperation since Year 465, have triangulated to a location approximately 40 kilometers from the current Pact Stones site. The goblin accounts describe the site as producing unusual effects on the ground surface — wrong shadows, sounds that did not match their sources, small objects that moved without being touched. The accounts are specific about the feeling of the site: "like something very large was paying attention and you were the thing it was paying attention to." The tradition keepers have been sharing these accounts with Guild researchers since Year 465. Every researcher who has read them has noted the same implication: the goblin tradition describes responsive terrain behavior in terms consistent with what Guild researchers have begun documenting inside the Rift itself. The location the tradition keepers describe is not the Pact Stones site. A Guild surface geological survey queued for Year 501 will attempt to confirm whether a subsurface anomaly consistent with a former wound migration endpoint exists at the triangulated location.

The tradition keepers knew about wound migration sites for 1,200 years before the Guild had a word for them. They have been mentioning it to every Guild researcher who asked since Year 465. The researchers filed the accounts correctly. The accounts are in the archive. They cross-reference directly against the Rift's documented responsive behavior. No policy has addressed this. The geological survey is scheduled. It has not yet run.

## Appendix A: Languages

Eight languages are spoken in the world as it exists in Year 500 Post-Atlas. Common is the administrative lingua franca of the Holy Dominion and, by extension, the default language of trade and inter-racial communication across most of the continent. It was standardized in Year 3 by the Dominion's administrative council as the empire's official language, which effectively meant that any non-Common speaker operating in imperial territory was at a structural disadvantage in any official context. The six native racial languages predate Common and continue in use within their communities; Draconic is the oldest documented language on the continent, with records predating human civilization.

Common is spoken by all races as a second language. Native speakers of each race's own language speak it with varying fluency; elves who administer the documentation system speak it with the specific technical precision of people who have been writing official documents in it for five hundred years. Lizardfolk speak it accurately and without affect. Goblins speak it fluently when they choose to and incomprehensibly when that is more useful.

| Language | Primary Speakers | Notes |
|----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Common** | All races (administrative lingua franca) | Standardized Year 3. Native to no race; first language in practice for many humans raised entirely in Dominion territory. |
| **Elvish** | Elves (native) | One of the oldest documented languages. Elvish has specific vocabulary for categories of loss, categories of long-duration observation, and categories of memory that Common cannot replicate without paraphrase. |

## Appendix B: Skills

Skills represent developed competencies that improve with practice. All skills begin at level 0 and advance to level 99 through use. Skill XP requirements scale linearly (100 XP times the current level per advance). Overall character level is a separate track that advances at 250 XP times the current level, with 10% of all skill XP contributing to overall level. Level-ups at character levels 10, 20, 30, and 40 unlock additional card slots. Stat points are gained every three character levels.

### Gathering Skills

Mining governs the extraction of stone, ore, and mineral resources from rock faces, quarry sites, and underground veins. Dwarves receive 25% bonus XP on this skill and will consistently outperform equivalent-effort investment from other races in pure material output. The skill's effectiveness at depth in both overworld dungeons and the Rift scales with level in ways that make high-level Mining one of the more valuable deep-dungeon competencies.

Woodcutting governs timber harvest from forested regions and the specific forest types found in biome-matched overworld dungeons. Output quality varies by biome — the Elven South's old-growth trees produce material properties that standard-biome timber does not, and the Wastes of Calidar's vitrified former forests produce glass-silica composites that require specific extraction techniques at high Woodcutting levels.

Farming governs cultivation of food crops, herbs, and materials from established plots. Requires plot ownership to operate at full capacity. Farming provides consistent material inputs for Cooking and Glassworking and scales in complexity as the skill advances — high-level Farming unlocks crop variants and cultivation techniques unavailable at lower levels.

Fishing governs resource extraction from water bodies, including the specific categories of underwater cave systems accessible to Lizardfolk characters with Aquatic Heritage. Base fishing skill is available to all races; Lizardfolk receive 15% bonus XP and access to fish species and resources in underwater zones that surface-access fishing cannot reach.

### Crafting Skills

Cooking converts raw food materials into consumables with gameplay effects — primarily healing, stamina restoration, and short-duration buff items. Recipe complexity scales with skill level. Camp cooking in the Rift, which requires both the Cooking skill and camp-kit access, produces items unavailable through standard crafting stations.

Glassworking converts glass-silica raw materials into lenses, vials, and component parts used in higher-tier crafting recipes. The skill has limited output applications at low levels and broad output applications at high levels, where it enables components for advanced scientific instruments, precision optics, and specific categories of magical artifact repair that no other crafting skill can produce.

Crafting is the general-purpose production skill covering metalwork, woodwork, stone construction, and the assembly-category recipes that require inputs from multiple material types. This skill's breadth makes it the default for players who want production capability without specializing; it does not match Dwarven Stone-Born Artisan specialization at high levels but serves every other race adequately.

Cogworking is the gnomish-adjacent engineering skill covering clockwork mechanisms, automaton construction, and mechanical system design. Gnomes receive 50% bonus XP and access to blueprint recipes not available to other races. Non-gnomish characters who invest heavily in Cogworking can approach gnomish output levels in standard mechanical items; they cannot access the Gnomish-exclusive blueprints without specific knowledge sources that are difficult to acquire and carefully tracked by the Collective.

### Combat Skills

Magic covers elemental, arcane, divine, and shadow magical practice. The magic ban does not prevent Guild members from training Magic in Guild-registered locations — the Compact's terms include a specific provision for Guild operational magic use that the Dominion accepted under protest and has disputed twice, unsuccessfully, since the Second Compact's ratification. Elves receive 50% bonus XP and 30% bonus unlocks on magic skill trees. Humans have a reduced effectiveness ceiling on magic skill trees as a consequence of the Doctrine-instilled cultural resistance to magical practice that has been embedded in human education since Year 3.

Melee covers blade, blunt, and martial combat. Three distinct sub-tracks covering different weapon classes and fighting styles. Orcs receive 25% bonus XP and 25% maximum HP that makes their sustained melee capacity the strongest of any race. The martial track includes unarmed combat techniques; Cat Folk receive these techniques as a racial ability independent of their Melee skill level, which can be improved through Melee investment to combine the racial technique with skill-level multipliers.

Archery covers ranged combat with bows, crossbows, and thrown weapons. Goblins receive 20% bonus XP on Archery as part of Guerrilla Instinct.

### Rogue Skills

Lockpicking governs the opening of locked containers, doors, and access points without a key. Scales in complexity with the lock quality encountered — standard imperial door locks at lower levels, Inquest security hardware at mid levels, and the specific categories of sealed access found in deep Rift floors at high levels. Goblins and Cat Folk both receive bonuses to this skill, making them the most effective lockpicking specialists available.

Thievery covers item acquisition through misdirection, pickpocketing, and distraction-based acquisition from both NPCs and environments. Goblins receive 20% bonus XP. The skill has social applications beyond direct item acquisition: high-level Thievery improves a character's reading of social environments in ways that provide dialogue options not available at lower levels.

### Social Skills

Coercion uses authority, implicit threat, or positional leverage to achieve compliance from NPC targets. Available to Human characters through Dominion Authority; accessible to other races through skill investment. Effectiveness varies by NPC type — Coercion works on bureaucratic NPCs, checkpoint officers, and institutional contacts; it is completely ineffective on lizardfolk sect members, experienced goblin resistance operatives, and anyone who has spent significant time in the Shadow Fen Commune.

Deception covers misdirection, false identity, and manipulative framing in social interactions. Available to Human characters through Dominion Authority. High-level Deception opens dialogue options that allow characters to navigate imperial zones with reduced documentation friction, engage with faction-hostile NPCs as neutral parties, and extract information from sources that would not share it with a direct approach. The skill's ceiling in the current political environment is high; the continent runs substantially on managed misrepresentation.

### Race-Locked Skills (Lizardfolk Only)

Ritual Magic, Water Rituals, and Blood Rituals are accessible exclusively to Lizardfolk characters. These skills cannot be traded, transferred, or otherwise made available to other races through any current in-game mechanism. Ritual Magic covers practices that predate the post-war magical tradition and operate through principles that the Inquest's documentation system cannot categorize, which is one of several reasons the Inquest's relationship with the lizardfolk sects is what it is. Water Rituals cover practices tied to the Hidden River Empire's aquatic knowledge base. Blood Rituals cover the most restricted tier of lizardfolk knowledge, require high Ritual Magic as a prerequisite, and produce effects that no other race's skill trees can replicate or counter reliably.

## Appendix C: The Card System

Cards are collectible items that provide stat bonuses, skill bonuses, racial trait enhancements, and special effects when equipped. They are obtained through card packs awarded at character level-ups (five to seven cards per pack), boss encounters (boss-floor kills in the Rift are flagged for pack rewards), specific NPC vendors at anchor towns, and player-to-player trade through the in-game auction house.

### Rarity Tiers

Eight rarity tiers exist. Drop rates are approximate — the system applies race-specific modifiers that shift individual draw distributions without altering the aggregate rates listed here.

| Tier | Base Drop Rate | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| **Common** | 45% | Base skill and stat cards. Useful but not distinctive; form the floor of any competent loadout. |
| **Uncommon** | 25% | Improved versions of Common effects; early access to skill sub-tree bonuses. |
| **Rare** | 15% | Skill tree specialization cards and the lower tier of faction-specific bonuses. |
| **Ultra Rare** | 8% | Significant stat packages and faction interaction bonuses. The threshold where card loadout starts defining playstyle in meaningful ways. |
| **Mythic Rare** | 4% | Major capability cards; racial enhancement amplifiers; cards that open quest lines when equipped. |
| **Legendary** | 2% | Each Legendary card is a distinct named item with a specific effect profile. No two Legendary cards do the same thing. |
| **Godly** | 0.8% | Named cards referencing specific historical figures, events, or entities. Their effects connect to the world's lore in ways that Common through Legendary cards do not. A Godly card depicting the Great Khan does something to melee effectiveness that cannot be replicated by combining lower-tier cards. |
| **Relic** | 0.2% | The rarest tier. Cards whose effects are not fully described in the standard card text — they have additional behavior that reveals itself over extended use. The card named "Heaven's Atlas" has been drawn three times in documented Guild history. All three holders are noted in the Guild's record. None of the three cards have identical behavior. |

### Card Styles

Each card can exist in one of five visual presentations. Style does not affect base card effects but applies multiplier bonuses to card value in NPC vendor and player auction transactions, and carries social weight among players for whom collection presentation matters.

Normal is the standard presentation. Holographic applies a light-refraction treatment to the card's surface that produces color-shift effects under different viewing angles. Golden applies metallic surface treatment in gold or bronze tones. Prismatic applies a multi-spectral surface treatment that produces full-spectrum color shift. Void applies a visual treatment that causes the card surface to appear to absorb rather than reflect light, producing a black that deepens at the card's edges. Void-style cards are significantly rarer than the same card in other styles.

### Race-Specific Draw Weighting

Elves have a 40% chance per draw of being assigned to the magic-tagged card pool before the standard rarity roll is applied. Goblins have a 35% chance per draw of being assigned to the stealth-tagged pool. Cat Folk have a 40% chance per draw of being assigned to the luck-tagged pool and additionally receive a 20% rarity bump chance that can promote any draw outcome one tier above its base result. Lizardfolk have a 25% chance per draw of being assigned to the ritual-tagged pool.

These weightings reflect the races' thematic identities in the game's card economy. They create meaningful decisions about whether to invest in building within the forced pool's strengths or to counter-invest in building outside it. An elf with extensive magic cards is the most capable magic practitioner in the game. An elf who invests specifically to reduce their magic-card weighting is pursuing a deliberate counter-build that has costs and benefits worth understanding before committing.

### Card Trading Restrictions

Cards representing racial innate traits — the water breathing and tremor sense of Lizardfolk Aquatic Heritage, the stone skin of Dwarven Stone-Born Artisan, the natural attack techniques of Cat Folk Pattern Recognition — cannot be traded to races without those traits. The effect is bound to the physiology it describes; a human cannot use a water-breathing card because the card's effect requires actual aquatic physiology to activate, and the card text makes this clear. Race-locked cards — Lizardfolk ritual magic cards — can be traded only to other Lizardfolk. Cards representing enhanced or modified versions of innate traits can be traded between members of the same race. All base skill, stat, and perk cards trade freely between any race.

### Card Fusion

Two cards of the same rarity tier can be fused to produce one card of the next rarity tier. The fusion product's effects reflect the combined base effects of the two source cards plus a 5% additive bonus that applies per fusion level in the card's lineage. A card that has been fused twice — a second-generation fusion product — carries its base effect plus 10% bonus (5% for each fusion in its lineage). Maximum two fusions per lineage: a card cannot be fused beyond the second generation. A second-generation fusion product at Legendary tier is the highest achievable single-card effect in the game. Fusing two Relic cards is theoretically possible by the system's logic and has not been observed. The Guild's card documentation archive has a standing open query on what a fused Relic card would do. No response has been filed.
